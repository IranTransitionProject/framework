## S17B-1) THE PROBLEM
Iran's regime operates one of the most sophisticated digital surveillance and censorship architectures in the world. As of February 2026:

- **National Information Network (NIN/SHOMA):** Splits Iranian digital space into a domestic intranet and the global internet. The domestic network is faster, cheaper, and comprehensively monitored. The global internet is available only at the regime's discretion and can be severed completely — as it was on January 8, 2026, and again today, February 28.
- **Two-tier access system:** Approximately 16,000 "white SIM cards" grant regime-connected individuals unrestricted global internet access. Exposed in November 2025 when X's location feature revealed regime officials posting from inside Iran without VPNs.
- **Mandatory digital identity:** SIM cards and devices linked to national identity numbers. Switching SIM cards or phones offers no protection — authorities can map social networks, identify organizers, and trace movements before, during, and after demonstrations.
- **Deep Packet Inspection (DPI):** Built with Russian (Protei) and Chinese (Huawei, ZTE) technical assistance. Allows selective throttling, user-level monitoring, and targeted disruption — more granular than blunt shutdowns.
- **IMSI catchers:** Deployed at protest sites (documented in Isfahan) to harvest device identifiers from anyone present.
- **SIAM program:** Centralized system for logging user behavior, tracking movements, and slowing targeted users' mobile data.
- **Facial recognition + CCTV:** Cross-referenced with biometric databases from mandatory digital identity cards.
- **Internet at 4% as of today** (Feb 28, 2026) due to military strikes. This follows the January 8 total blackout and the ongoing 50% reduction since February 16.

**The core constraint:** Any communication architecture for Iranian civil society must assume that the regime has the technical capability to monitor, intercept, identify, and geolocate participants — and that total network severance is a standing capability deployed routinely.

This is a harder problem than any of the five historical cases below faced.

## S17B-2) CASE STUDY 1: POLAND — SOLIDARITY (1980-1989)
### Communication Architecture

**Underground press (bibuła/drugi obieg):**
- The backbone of Solidarity's survival after martial law (December 1981). Between 1976 and 1990, approximately 3,000-4,000 independent periodicals and over 6,000 books and pamphlets were published underground.
- The most influential publication, *Tygodnik Mazowsze* (Mazovian Weekly), operated from "floating offices" — editors changed apartments every two weeks, production facilities every four weeks.
- At peak, 3 in 4 respondents in a 1985 Kraków survey reported reading underground publications (26% regularly, 47% often).
- Radio Solidarity began broadcasting April 12, 1982, despite martial law.

**External support channels:**
- AFL-CIO under Lane Kirkland provided sustained material support — printing equipment, communications gear, funding.
- CIA Operation QUHELPFUL (officially from November 1982): smuggled fax machines, printing presses, transmitters, telephones, shortwave radios. Total expenditure under $20 million over 8 years.
- CIA deliberately built parallel networks rather than using existing Solidarity structures — to avoid compromising the movement if CIA channels were penetrated.
- Catholic Church served as both physical sanctuary and smuggling conduit (Vatican involvement coordinated directly with Pope John Paul II).
- Multiple redundant smuggling routes established — when one was compromised, others continued.

**Compartmentalization:**
- "The least they knew, the better off they and their networks were, so if captured, they could not betray others."
- Decentralized structure by design — hundreds of small publishers rather than a few large ones.
- Regional chapters had independent production capacity. Central coordination existed but was not single-point-of-failure.

### Key Lessons for Iran

| Solidarity Feature | Iran Applicability | Constraint |
|---|---|---|
| Physical underground press | Limited — Iran's crisis is digital-era; physical distribution slower but surveillance-resistant | Regime controls paper/printing supply chains less than digital infrastructure |
| Floating offices / rotating production | Directly applicable — operational security through constant movement | Iran's biometric ID + CCTV makes physical movement trackable in ways 1980s Poland was not |
| Multiple redundant external supply channels | Critical — single channel = single point of failure | Iran's border security tighter than Cold War Poland; digital channels can substitute for physical smuggling |
| CIA maintained separate networks from movement | Important principle — external support should not compromise internal structures | Regime propaganda already frames all opposition as CIA/Mossad agents; foreign channel exposure is lethal |
| Church as sanctuary institution | Iran lacks equivalent — mosques are regime-controlled; no independent institutional shelter | Some Sunni/Sufi/minority religious networks may offer limited analog |
| Decentralized production | Core principle — no central node whose destruction collapses the system | Digital decentralization is technically possible but requires literacy |

**Critical difference:** Solidarity operated in a surveillance environment orders of magnitude less sophisticated than Iran 2026. Poland's secret police could tap phones and follow individuals but could not monitor the entire population's digital activity simultaneously. Iran can.

## S17B-3) CASE STUDY 2: SOUTH AFRICA — ANC / OPERATION VULA (1986-1990)
### Communication Architecture

**Operation Vula — the gold standard of exile-internal secure communication:**
- Initiated 1986, launched inside South Africa 1988. Purpose: smuggle banned ANC leaders back into the country and establish underground network linking domestic activists with exile headquarters.
- Tim Jenkin designed the communications system. Requirements: safe to use from public phones/radios; real-time connection between underground and HQ; ability to send detailed reports and store documents securely.
- The system provided "almost real-time connection between activists underground and those based at HQ" — described as achieving "more in two years than what had been achieved in the previous 20 years. All because of good, secure communications."
- Critically: connected Mandela in prison to ANC headquarters. Messages smuggled in/out by Mandela's lawyer, transmitted to Tambo through Vula's communication system. The regime "thought they had him isolated... but meanwhile, he was actually sending long messages through the system."

**Security architecture:**
- "Strictly need-to-know basis" — administered as a "President's Project" through Tambo's office. Even the ANC's own National Executive Committee did not receive reports.
- When Maharaj left Lusaka to establish Vula inside South Africa, ANC members were told he was going to Europe for medical treatment.
- Financed through front companies and dummy bank accounts.
- Logistical support from European anti-apartheid groups (especially Netherlands).
- Highly secret until uncovered in 1990 — even then, the communications system itself was never compromised; the operation was exposed through a police raid that found documents.

**Parallel structures — UDF as above-ground complement:**
- The United Democratic Front (UDF) and COSATU operated legally (until banned), providing mass mobilization while ANC underground handled strategic coordination.
- "Freelance" underground operatives — individuals who supported the ANC but were not formal members, maintaining plausible deniability. Involved in harboring soldiers, smuggling weapons and literature, feeding cadres.
- Four Pillars: armed struggle, underground structures, popular mobilization, international isolation — each operating through different channels with different security profiles.

**Infiltration countermeasures:**
- Extensive state infiltration documented — agents provocateurs, moles, booby-trapped weapons caches.
- Counter-intelligence was a permanent function, not an afterthought.
- "Freelance" operatives who deliberately avoided formal ANC association provided a layer of operational security — they couldn't betray organizational structure they didn't know.

### Key Lessons for Iran

| Vula Feature | Iran Applicability | Constraint |
|---|---|---|
| Dedicated secure communication system designed from scratch | Critical need — Iran needs a purpose-built system, not improvised use of commercial tools | Iran's DPI and device-level surveillance exceed apartheid South Africa's capabilities |
| Need-to-know compartmentalization even within own organization | Directly applicable — factional complexity of Iranian opposition makes this both necessary and difficult | Multiple opposition factions (MEK, monarchists, republicans, ethnic groups) lack trust for shared infrastructure |
| Exile-internal linkage as primary strategic function | Core requirement — diaspora has resources and freedom; internal movement has legitimacy and numbers | Iran regime actively targets diaspora (assassinations, cyber-operations, family threats) |
| "Freelance" operatives with no formal organizational link | Highly applicable — Iran's movement is largely organic, not centrally organized | Provides natural compartmentalization but makes coordination harder |
| Above-ground/below-ground complementary structures | Essential — legal civil society (lawyers, journalists) provides cover for underground coordination | Iran has systematically destroyed above-ground civil society (Alikordi assassination, Dec 2025) |
| Communication system never technically compromised | Gold standard — exposure came through physical documents, not crypto breach | Iran's technical capabilities for intercepting digital communications are more advanced |

**Critical difference:** Operation Vula succeeded because the apartheid regime's communications surveillance, while formidable, could not perform the kind of real-time mass digital monitoring Iran deploys. Vula used analog-era encrypted communications over public phone networks — a technique that has no direct digital equivalent under Iran's NIN architecture.

## S17B-4) CASE STUDY 3: CHILE — VICARIATE OF SOLIDARITY (1976-1992)
### Communication Architecture

**Institutional shelter model:**
- The Vicariate of Solidarity operated as an agency of the Catholic Church under the Archdiocese of Santiago. This institutional affiliation provided legal and moral protection that no secular organization could achieve.
- People used secret passages to enter the Vicariate from the adjacent Cathedral to avoid surveillance.
- Provided legal services to 250,000+ people. Opened cases documenting human rights abuse. Records of 19,000+ cases later used by truth commissions.
- Published biweekly magazine *Solidaridad* with 33,000 circulation — combining evangelical content with unemployment statistics, malnutrition data, and human rights documentation.
- By the 1980s, the Vicariate and Catholic Church were the two most trusted institutions in Chile — ranking above all political parties, media, and government bodies.

**Documentation as resistance:**
- The Vicariate's core function was not communication in the conventional sense but *documentation* — creating an authoritative, legally admissible record of regime crimes.
- This documentation served dual purpose: immediate legal defense of detainees, and long-term evidentiary foundation for post-transition justice.
- The regime recognized this threat and attacked: jailed the Vicariate's chief doctor, prosecuted nine of its lawyers, attempted to subpoena financial records.

**Broader opposition coordination:**
- Chile's opposition organized around the 1988 plebiscite — a mechanism the regime itself created (1980 constitution mandated a vote on Pinochet's continuation).
- 16-party coalition mobilized voters, poll watchers, and demonstrators. Nonviolent creativity: slowdowns, boycotts, public singing, symbolic actions that "baffled the regime."
- 15-minute television spots (granted under plebiscite rules) revealed regime abuses to national audience.
- Church-based SERPAJ cultivated network of trained activists and developed protest strategy.
- Opposition drew inspiration from both Gandhi and Poland's Solidarity movement.

**Arpilleras — communication through art:**
- The Vicariate funded workshops where women (especially relatives of the disappeared) created textile art depicting life under dictatorship.
- Arpilleras were sold abroad — serving as both income for families and international communication of Chilean reality.
- One of few self-expression avenues the regime could not easily suppress.

### Key Lessons for Iran

| Chile Feature | Iran Applicability | Constraint |
|---|---|---|
| Institutional shelter (Church) | Iran has no equivalent independent institution with comparable moral authority and legal protection | Shi'a clerical establishment IS the regime; no independent religious institution exists |
| Documentation as primary function | Critical — HRANA already performs this function for Iran; model validates approach | Iran's internet blackouts prevent real-time documentation; post-blackout recovery is key |
| Plebiscite as organizing mechanism | Not applicable — Iran has no constitutional mechanism for regime referendum | Could inform post-transition constitutional design |
| Art as communication channel | Applicable — cultural production as resistance has deep Iranian roots (poetry, film, music) | Digital cultural production is monitored; physical forms (graffiti, samizdat art) more surveillance-resistant |
| Coalition building across ideological lines | Critical need — Iran's opposition is fragmented (monarchists, republicans, ethnic groups, leftists) | Chile's 16-party coalition succeeded despite deep ideological differences — model for Iran |
| Long-term evidentiary documentation for post-transition justice | Essential — current documentation work by HRANA, IranWire, Amnesty creates accountability foundation | Must be preserved outside Iran; regime can destroy internal records |

**Critical difference:** Chile's Vicariate could exist because Pinochet, despite his brutality, maintained some institutional constraints — he did not shut down the Catholic Church. Iran's theocratic regime has no such institutional gap; the religious establishment is the regime. The closest Iranian analog would be if a network of senior clerics broke with the regime and provided institutional shelter — a development that Brief #7 (Larijani variable) tracks but that has not occurred.

## S17B-5) CASE STUDY 4: BALTIC STATES — SINGING REVOLUTION (1987-1991)
### Communication Architecture

**Popular front organizations as coordinating bodies:**
- Estonian Rahvarinne, Latvian Tautas Fronte, Lithuanian Sajūdis — mass membership organizations that emerged under Gorbachev's glasnost.
- Sajūdis attracted support from the Lithuanian diaspora in the United States, which provided money, equipment, and access to journalists and US government officials.
- Lithuania's Chronicle of the Catholic Church, published underground from 1972 to 1988 without interruption, documented human rights violations for 16 years before the popular fronts emerged.

**Cross-border coordination:**
- Baltic Assembly organized regular meetings among leaderships of all three movements.
- The Baltic Way (675km human chain, 2 million participants) was planned in under six weeks through trilateral coordination.
- Information disseminated through regional organizations and Radio Free Europe, which broadcast through Soviet jammers.
- Lithuanian Radio (formally Communist Party-controlled) played a crucial role in coordinating the Baltic Way — local authorities silently condoned the action by refraining from interference.

**Cultural resistance as communication:**
- The "Singing Revolution" — mass gatherings where banned national anthems and folk songs were performed as political protest.
- Traditional costumes, pre-occupation flags, and cultural symbols served as non-verbal political communication.
- Cultural events provided cover for political organizing — the regime could not easily ban singing.

**Diaspora role:**
- Black Ribbon Day protests in Western cities (1980s) maintained international attention on Baltic occupation.
- Diaspora communities in US, Canada, Sweden provided funding, diplomatic access, and international amplification.
- August 23 anniversary of Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact provided recurring mobilization date with historical legitimacy.

### Key Lessons for Iran

| Baltic Feature | Iran Applicability | Constraint |
|---|---|---|
| Mass membership organizations | Iran's movement is organic and leaderless — no Sajūdis equivalent exists | Regime has systematically prevented formation of organized opposition |
| Cross-border coordination among parallel movements | Potentially applicable to Iranian ethnic movements (Kurdish, Baluch, Azerbaijani, Arab) | These movements have different goals; coordination requires trust-building |
| Radio Free Europe as external broadcast | Existing: Iran International, BBC Persian, VOA Persian, Manoto serve this function | Regime blocks satellite dishes; streaming requires internet access |
| Cultural events as political cover | Deep Iranian tradition — Nowruz, mourning ceremonies, poetry gatherings | Regime surveils cultural gatherings; mourning cycles (chehelom) already weaponized politically |
| Diaspora as funding/diplomatic channel | Large, wealthy, politically active Iranian diaspora (especially US, UK, Germany, Canada) | Diaspora is fragmented; regime uses family hostage-taking to silence diaspora activism |
| Recurring historical dates as mobilization anchors | Nowruz, chehelom cycles, anniversary of Mahsa Amini's death, Jan 8 massacre | Regime pre-deploys security forces around predictable dates |
| Glasnost opening exploited by movements | No equivalent — Iran has no reformist faction with Gorbachev-level systemic opening power | Pezeshkian's "reform" presidency was cosmetic; no structural opening occurred |

**Critical difference:** The Baltic movements succeeded in a context where the central imperial authority (Moscow) was weakening and chose not to use overwhelming force. Iran's regime has shown willingness to use maximum force (January 2026 massacre, 5,000-20,000 estimated killed). The Baltic model assumes a regime that can be pressured into restraint; Iran's cannot, at least under current conditions. The post-strike environment may change this calculus.

## S17B-6) CASE STUDY 5: MYANMAR — NUG/PDF (2021-PRESENT)
### Communication Architecture

**Cellular, horizontally networked resistance:**
- After the February 2021 coup, resistance forces organized as "a cellular, horizontally networked guerrilla force" — not by design choice but by necessity.
- Young protesters traveled to border areas controlled by ethnic armed organizations (EAOs) for military training, then returned to form local People's Defense Forces (PDFs).
- PDFs formed in 250 out of 330 townships, with some townships having up to 2,000 coordinated members.
- People's Defense Teams (PDTs) specialize in urban guerrilla warfare, training, logistics, and mobilization.

**Command coordination challenges:**
- NUG formed Central Command and Coordination Committee (C3C) in October 2021 to unify disparate groups.
- Separate Joint Command and Coordination (J2C) for Karen territory.
- Despite formal structures, "strategic coordination mechanisms are sparse, and operational communication is rudimentary at best."
- Internal tensions between NUG (Bamar-dominated) and ethnic armed organizations with different political goals.
- 65,000 NUG-PDF fighters + 135,000 troops from 20+ EAOs — large but fragmented force.

**Intelligence architecture:**
- NUG Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA) provides intelligence support for C3C.
- Military Intelligence Department under PDF high command with combat intelligence, psychological warfare, counter-intelligence, and technical intelligence functions.
- Assassination of informers (dalan) is routine — both sides treat intelligence war as existential.

**Technology adaptation under constraint:**
- Modified commercial drones for ISR and precision strikes.
- Homemade small arms, IEDs, improvised systems.
- "Necessity is the mother of invention" — resource scarcity drives tactical innovation.
- Underground channels for arms acquisition; NUG declines to reveal weapons sources.

### Key Lessons for Iran

| Myanmar Feature | Iran Applicability | Constraint |
|---|---|---|
| Cellular horizontal network emerging organically | Iran's protest movement already operates this way — leaderless, decentralized, organic | Provides resilience against decapitation but makes strategic coordination nearly impossible |
| Border areas as training/staging zones | Iran's Kurdish, Baluch regions + cross-border access to Iraq Kurdistan, Pakistan, Turkey | Regime has heavily militarized border zones; cross-border movement extremely dangerous |
| EAO alliance model | Iran's ethnic armed groups (PJAK Kurdish, Jundallah Baluch) are potential allies | Regime frames ethnic movements as separatist; alliance carries territorial integrity risks |
| Technology improvisation | Iranian engineers and tech workers have high technical capability | Regime's DPI and device tracking make digital innovation riskier than Myanmar's less sophisticated surveillance |
| Intelligence war as permanent feature | Already the reality — regime intelligence agencies vs. opposition OSINT/digital forensics | Iran's intelligence apparatus (MOIS + IRGC Intel) is far more capable than Myanmar's Tatmadaw intelligence |
| Fragmentation as simultaneous weakness and resilience | Iran's opposition fragmentation prevents coordination but also prevents total decapitation | Need mechanisms for coordination that don't require organizational unity |

**Critical difference:** Myanmar's resistance had access to decades-old ethnic armed organization infrastructure — established territorial control, training facilities, weapons stockpiles, and cross-border supply lines. Iran's opposition has none of this infrastructure. The Iranian movement is overwhelmingly civilian and urban, operating in the regime's strongest territory rather than from ungoverned peripheries.

## S17B-7) CROSS-CASE SYNTHESIS: THE CHANNEL-TASK MATCHING FRAMEWORK
### What These Cases Tell Us About Iran

**Universal principles confirmed across all five cases:**

1. **Compartmentalization is non-negotiable.** Every successful resistance communication system limited information access on a strict need-to-know basis. Solidarity's decentralized publishers, Vula's "President's Project" classification, Chile's freelance operatives, Baltic rotating production — all confirm that security scales inversely with centralization.

2. **Multiple redundant channels prevent single-point-of-failure.** CIA deliberately built parallel networks to Solidarity's own. ANC maintained separate communication, intelligence, and logistical channels. Baltic movements coordinated across three countries precisely because one country's compromise wouldn't collapse the others.

3. **External-internal linkage is the critical capability.** Every case depended on connecting those inside the repressive state with resources, amplification, and coordination outside it. Solidarity's Western labor union support, ANC's Lusaka headquarters, Chile's international solidarity network, Baltic diaspora communities, Myanmar's NUG-in-exile.

4. **Documentation is a strategic weapon, not just a humanitarian function.** Chile's Vicariate created the evidentiary foundation that outlasted Pinochet. This is HRANA's current role for Iran — and it must be preserved and expanded regardless of other priorities.

5. **Cultural communication channels resist suppression better than political ones.** Baltic singing, Chilean arpilleras, Solidarity's underground literature, Iranian poetry and film traditions — all exploit the regime's difficulty in banning culture without appearing absurd.

6. **Above-ground/below-ground complementarity is essential.** Legal civil society organizations provide cover, resources, and legitimacy that underground networks cannot generate alone. Iran's systematic destruction of above-ground civil society (lawyers murdered, journalists imprisoned, NGOs banned) makes this the hardest principle to implement.

### What Makes Iran Uniquely Difficult

| Challenge | Historical Precedent | Iran Reality |
|---|---|---|
| Surveillance sophistication | 1980s analog surveillance (wiretaps, informers, physical following) | Real-time digital mass surveillance (DPI, IMSI catchers, biometric ID, facial recognition, device tracking) |
| Network severance capability | Partial (jamming radio, raiding print shops) | Total (can and does reduce internet to 0-4%) |
| Institutional shelter | Catholic Church (Poland, Chile, Lithuania) | No independent institution — religious establishment IS the regime |
| Organized opposition structure | Solidarity (10M members), ANC (exile HQ), Sajūdis | Leaderless, organic, fragmented — by design and by repressive necessity |
| External safe haven | Zambia (ANC), Western Europe (Solidarity), US (Baltic diaspora) | Iranian diaspora in democracies — but regime conducts extraterritorial operations against them |
| Regime willingness to use force | Variable — Poland's martial law was restrained; Chile's Pinochet killed thousands; South Africa killed hundreds; Myanmar kills thousands | Iran: 5,000-20,000 killed January 2026; 50,000+ detained; 1988 mass execution precedent |
| Border access | ANC had Mozambique, Angola; Baltic had Finland, Sweden proximity; Myanmar has Thailand, India | Iran's borders heavily militarized; Kurdish and Baluch regions offer limited access |

### The Iran-Specific Problem Set

Iran's communication security architecture must solve for a unique combination of constraints that no single historical case addresses:

1. **Total network severance** — the regime can and will cut all internet connectivity. No historical case faced this except Myanmar (where connectivity was degraded but not eliminated to the same degree). Solutions must include non-internet channels.

2. **Device-level identification** — SIM cards tied to national ID, IMSI catchers at protest sites, mandatory digital identity. Solutions must account for the fact that carrying a phone to a protest = being identified.

3. **No institutional shelter** — unlike every other case, Iran has no independent institution (church, university, labor union) that the regime respects enough to leave alone. Solutions cannot assume protected physical spaces.

4. **Diaspora under threat** — unlike Baltic or Polish diasporas operating safely in Western democracies, Iranian diaspora faces extraterritorial intelligence operations, assassination plots, and family hostage-taking. Solutions must protect diaspora participants as well as internal ones.

5. **Factional fragmentation** — the opposition is divided among monarchists, republicans, ethnic autonomy movements, Marxists, and liberal democrats. No Solidarity-style unified movement exists. Solutions must enable coordination without requiring organizational unity.

## S17B-9) RESEARCH GAPS — FOR NEXT SESSION
1. **Mesh networking under Iranian conditions:** Has Briar/Bridgefy been tested in Iran? What is the effective range? Can the regime detect mesh network traffic? What happened during the January 2026 blackout — did any mesh networks function?

2. **Starlink/satellite options:** McCormick-Rosen bill (introduced Feb 24, 2026) specifically addresses satellite-based communications for Iran. What is the current state of Starlink terminal detection and confiscation in Iran? Are there other satellite communication options?

3. **Analog fallback channels:** What physical communication methods (carrier networks, dead drops, coded signals) are viable in Iran's urban environment? What is the courier network capacity across Iran's borders?

4. **OPSEC training distribution:** How do you teach digital security to millions of people who cannot currently access the internet? Physical pamphlets? Pre-loaded USB drives? Training-of-trainers models?

5. **Factional coordination mechanisms:** How did Chile's 16-party coalition coordinate despite ideological differences? What was the minimum institutional infrastructure required? Can this model be adapted for Iran's fragmented opposition?

6. **Post-blackout documentation recovery:** When Iranian internet restores, there will be a massive wave of documentation from inside. What infrastructure needs to be pre-positioned to receive, verify, and preserve this evidence?

## S17B-10) CONNECTION TO CURRENT EVENTS (Feb 28, 2026)
The strikes happening right now make this research urgent in three ways:

1. **The 50,000 detainees** are in maximum danger during the blackout. Any communication capability that could reach inside Iran's prisons — or at least document what happens when the blackout lifts — has immediate humanitarian value.

2. **Post-strike chaos may create openings.** If the IRGC is degraded (Variable 1 in the monitoring tracker), surveillance infrastructure may temporarily weaken. Historical precedent: every regime transition creates a window where surveillance systems are disrupted before new ones are established. The question is whether civil society is prepared to exploit that window.

3. **The Brief #10 hollow deal trap** requires an organized civil society voice that can articulate conditionality demands. Without communication architecture, the diaspora speaks but the internal population cannot — and the regime negotiates on behalf of people it is killing.

**Trigger for 17-B deployment:** When Iranian internet begins restoring above 20%. The research compiled here is ready to be structured into actionable guidance at that point.
