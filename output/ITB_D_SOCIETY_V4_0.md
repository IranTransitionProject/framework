Iranian society in 2025–2026 operates across five functional strata. These are not rigid — movement between them is constant, mostly downward.

| Stratum | Est. Pop. | Monthly Income | Key Characteristics |
|---------|-----------|----------------|---------------------|

| **Upper middle class** | ~8–10M | $450–650+/mo reported (but often supplemented) | Urban professionals in Tehran/Isfahan, dual-income families, those with hard-currency access, diaspora remittance recipients. Brain drain source. |

**Sources:** Wikipedia (Social class in Iran, updated 2025); IMF; Majlis Research Center; Iran International; Atlantic Council. [Med confidence — no transparent income census]


### _L71) D5.2 The Collapse of the Middle Class
The most consequential social change in Iran over the past decade is the systematic erosion of the middle class. Academic research (Farzanegan and Habibi, 2025, European Journal of Political Economy) estimates sanctions alone reduced the middle class by 28 percentage points between 2012 and 2019. [Fact — High, peer-reviewed]

This is not merely an economic statistic. The middle class historically drove Iran's political and social development — from the Constitutional Revolution (1905–1911) through the reform movement. Its collapse means:
* The protest demographic has shifted from middle-class cultural demands to lower-class economic desperation. [Inference — Med]
* The Dec 2025 uprising was triggered by bazaaris (economic actors), not students (cultural actors). This represents a structural shift. [Fact — Med]
* A weaker middle class reduces the constituency for moderate, negotiated transitions. [Inference — Med, supported by Atlantic Council analysis]

The old analytical framework — lower class = economic grievances, middle class = political demands — is no longer valid. Both strata now simultaneously experience economic deprivation, political marginalization, and cultural frustration. [Inference — Med, Iran News Update / state-affiliated media acknowledgment]


### _L82) D5.3 The Gini Coefficient Reality Check
Iran's official Gini coefficient of ~0.387 (SCI, 2024–2025) looks moderate — similar to upper-middle-income countries. This is misleading because:
* It measures reported income, not actual wealth. The top tier's income is substantially unreported. [Inference — Med]
* It does not capture IRGC/bonyad/Setad economic control (25–60% of GDP depending on estimate). [Inference — Med]
* Targeted cash transfers (subsidy reform) artificially suppress the Gini by several points while not addressing structural inequality. [Inference — Med, academic sources]

## D8) CORRUPTION AS MORAL ECONOMY — NEW SECTION
### _L170) D8.1 The User's Thesis: "Not Worse Than Turkey"
On the Transparency International CPI, Iran (23) scores significantly worse than Turkey (31). Both are in the "highly corrupt" tier. But this institutional metric obscures a critical distinction:

**Elite/systemic corruption:** Iran is structurally worse than Turkey. The IRGC, bonyads, EIKO/Setad, and semi-public sector constitute a designed kleptocracy that Turkey lacks at the same scale. The $21B Mobarakeh Steel fraud, the $3B Sarmayeh Bank fraud, the $3.4B Debsh Tea scandal, the €6.6B petrochemical diversion — these are regime-orchestrated extraction at a level Turkey's AKP corruption does not reach. [Fact — Med]

**Everyday/survival corruption:** Iran is comparable to Turkey. The teacher who pays a small facilitation fee to expedite paperwork, the business owner who pays a known rate to clear customs, the family that uses connections (parti-bazi) to get a hospital appointment — this is structurally identical to Turkish rüşvet and torpil culture. [Inference — Med]

**Why this distinction matters for transition:**
* Elite corruption demands accountability (lustration, asset recovery, judicial process). Without it, the new order has no legitimacy.
* Everyday corruption demands *systems reform* (digitization, transparent processes, competitive salaries), not punishment. Criminalizing survival-level corruption would target millions of ordinary people who had no real choice.
* Conflating the two — treating the clerk who took $20 the same as the general who stole $2 billion — is the mistake Iraq made with de-Baathification. [Inference — High confidence on the Iraq comparison]


### _L183) D8.2 Structure of Everyday Corruption
The corruption ecosystem operates at identifiable scales:

**Petty facilitation (parti-bazi / connections):** Universal. Getting a government appointment, hospital bed, school enrollment, traffic ticket dismissal. Functions as an informal tax. Most Iranians do not morally condemn this in others — they see it as navigating a broken system. [Inference — Med]

**Regulatory rent-seeking:** Building permits, business licenses, import clearances. Known rate structures exist. Businesses budget for this. Comparable to Turkey, Egypt, and much of the Middle East. [Inference — Med]

**Professional moonlighting:** Doctors, professors, teachers, government employees holding second and third jobs, often in the informal economy. This is technically corruption (using state-credentialed expertise in unofficial capacity) but it is universal because state salaries are below subsistence. Over 60% of workers hold multiple jobs. [Fact — Med, NCRI citing Iranian sources]

**Sanctions-driven informality:** The sanctions regime creates a corruption necessity. Importing medical equipment, food, technology — all require navigating unofficial channels that are technically illegal. This structural corruption is driven by external policy, not internal moral failure. [Inference — Med]


### _L195) D8.3 The Hashem Pesaran Finding
Prominent Iranian economist Hashem Pesaran estimated that over a 30-year period, only 20% of Iran's economic malaise was caused by sanctions; 80% was due to domestic policies. [Fact — Med, cited in Atlantic Council]

This is important: it means the corruption problem is primarily structural (regime design), not primarily external (sanctions). A post-sanctions Iran would still face massive corruption unless institutional reform accompanies political change.

## D14) POLITICAL CULTURE: THE ARBAB MENTALITY, TAXATION, AND RULE OF LAW — NEW SECTION
This section addresses a deep structural dimension of Iranian society that most Western analyses completely miss: the dominant popular understanding of what government *is*, what taxes *mean*, and what law *does*. These attitudes, shaped by centuries of landlord-peasant relations, a century of oil rentierism, and 45 years of theocratic kleptocracy, constitute perhaps the single greatest obstacle to building a functioning post-transition state — greater even than security threats or economic collapse, because they operate at the level of unconscious assumption rather than conscious choice.

### _L305) D14.1 The Arbab Mental Model: Government as Owner, Not Servant
**Historical Roots.** The arbāb-ra'īyatī (landlord-peasant) system defined Iranian social organization for centuries. The arbab was not merely an employer; he was the *owner of the village itself*, a patriarchal authority whose obligations to peasants were fixed by custom rather than contract (Encyclopaedia Iranica). Key features that persist psychologically:

- **Absentee control:** Large landowners lived in cities, managing through stewards (mobāšer). Peasants related to a distant, invisible authority whose decisions were final and whose motives were opaque. This maps directly onto how most Iranians experience government today.
- **Mutual obligation without contract:** The arbab was *expected* to provide — seed, water access, protection, dispute resolution — and in return received labor and a share of production. But this was custom, not negotiated right. The peasant had no leverage; the arbab's generosity was discretionary.
- **Trickle-down as the only model:** Wealth flowed downward from the owner. It was not *earned* through a market, nor *taxed* by a state providing services. It was *distributed* by an authority who *owned* the productive capacity.

**Land Reform Did Not Kill the Mental Model.** The Shah's White Revolution (1960s) redistributed land to 1.5 million peasant families, but the psychic structure survived for several reasons:

1. The Shah himself became the new arbab — the modernizing patron who would distribute oil wealth and transform Iran from above. His slogan was literally "Shah and People Revolution."
2. Oil revenues, which freed the state from needing to tax citizens after the mid-1960s, reinforced the pattern: wealth flows down from the owner (now the state), not up from productive citizens.
3. The Islamic Revolution replicated the model perfectly: Khomeini as the spiritual arbab, the revolution carried out "in the name of the oppressed" (mostaz'afin), with an explicit promise that the new order would redistribute wealth to the dispossessed. The 1979 Constitution enshrines the government's obligation to confiscate ill-gotten wealth and redistribute it.
4. Post-revolution subsidies, cash transfers, and bonyad welfare systems all reinforce the arbab model: the state *provides*, the citizen *receives*.

**Result:** For a significant portion of the population — especially those over 50 who grew up under either the Shah's paternalism or Khomeini's revolutionary welfare promises — *government* fundamentally means *the entity that owns the national wealth and decides how much trickles down*. This is not a political position; it is an unconscious framework. The concept of government as a service provider funded by citizen taxation, accountable to those citizens, is intellectually understood by the educated urban class but has *no lived experiential basis* for the majority.


### _L322) D14.2 The Rentier Psychology: Oil Broke the Fiscal Contract Before It Could Form
Iran's rentier state dynamic was so foundational that the academic concept itself was invented to describe Iran (Hossein Mahdavy, 1970). Key implications:

**No Taxation–Representation Nexus.** Since the mid-1960s, the Iranian state has not depended on citizen taxation for its primary revenue. Oil exports provided the external rent that funded the state. This severed the fundamental mechanism through which citizens in other countries developed expectations of accountability: *I pay, therefore I have a say.* Iran's citizens never developed this expectation at scale because they were never systematically taxed at scale.

**Tax Revenue Remains Marginal.** As of 2024-2025:
- Tax revenues (excluding customs): ~7% of GDP, vs. 25-30% in developed countries
- 40% of economic activity is legally tax-exempt (per Expediency Council)
- An additional 21% operates off-the-books entirely
- Combined: 60%+ of economic activity avoids or evades taxation
- Tax evasion costs: estimated $12-20 billion/year
- Top 10% of earners pay only 3% of income taxes (vs. 70%+ in the US)
- Half of Iran's billionaires pay *no* taxes at all (former INTA head, 2022)

**The Subsidy Entitlement.** Subsidies have functioned as the rentier state's substitute for a social contract:
- Pre-reform subsidies consumed ~25% of GDP (2009)
- Energy subsidies alone: $69 billion in 2018 — 15.3% of GDP, 16% of *global* energy subsidies
- Ahmadinejad's 2010 subsidy reform replaced cheap goods with cash payments; 95% of Iranians signed up despite government requests for the wealthy to abstain
- Every attempt to reduce subsidies has triggered unrest: Rafsanjani (1990s riots), Khatami (blocked in parliament), Ahmadinejad (gas station attacks 2007), Rouhani (2019 "Bloody Aban" — 1,500 killed), and the 2025-2026 protests partly triggered by subsidy/exchange-rate restructuring

The subsidized price is not perceived as a government benefit — it is perceived as a *citizen's right*. One Iranian commenter captured this perfectly: "Paying a reduced rate for energy & utilities is the RIGHT of every Iranian whose wealth, mostly from oil income and partly taxes, has gone into building these utilities." This is the arbab model translated into modern economics: the national wealth *belongs to* the people, and the government's role is to *distribute* it, not to *earn* it through taxation.

**January 2026: The $7 Coupon.** The latest iteration: after eliminating subsidized exchange rates, the government proposed monthly coupons worth one million tomans (~$7) to every Iranian — a direct cash-for-loyalty transfer that the newspaper Setareh Sobh called an "economic gamble," noting that similar efforts have repeatedly failed.


### _L347) D14.3 Rule of Law: The Zerangi Culture
Perhaps the most operationally significant cultural dimension for transition planning is the deeply ingrained attitude toward law itself.

**"Smart People Don't Follow the Law."** A landmark study of Iranian legal culture (Brännström et al., Iranian Studies, 2018) found:
- Senior Iranian lawyers report that "Iranians are not law-abiding" and that "the general belief is that smart (zerang) people don't follow the law"
- Clients prefer to pay bribes over legal fees — even when told bribes are unreliable — because they have more faith in corrupt personal connections than in legal process
- When Iranians do retain lawyers, it is often "because they believe lawyers know how to *sidestep* the law," not to use legal process as intended
- Iranian television consistently portrays lawyers as criminal accomplices, never as defenders of justice (unless they are implausibly pious clerical figures)
- Government officials' visible contempt for law teaches citizens that law is a tool of power, not a framework of justice

**Zerangi as Cultural Value.** The concept of zerangi (cleverness/shrewdness/cunning) occupies a central and ambivalent place in Iranian culture:
- Folk heroes in Iranian stories are zerang characters who outwit authority — the merchant who tricks the governor, the peasant who deceives the arbab
- Zerangi is admired when directed against oppressive authority (a survival skill under arbitrary rule) but becomes corrosive when generalized to all social interaction
- Academic research (Taheri, 2015; Bateson et al., 1977) identifies zerangi as a key "ideal character type" — the resourceful, clever person who overcomes overwhelming odds through wit rather than through institutional process
- In political affairs, zerangi manifests as playing both sides, spreading rumors, maximizing advantage through information asymmetry — behaviors that are rational adaptations to centuries of arbitrary rule but devastating for civic institution-building

**The Vicious Cycle.** The research identifies a self-reinforcing system:
1. Government officials don't follow law → Citizens lose trust in legal institutions
2. Citizens circumvent law → Government relies on coercion instead of consent
3. Coercion replaces process → Citizens further invest in zerangi as survival strategy
4. Universal distrust → Lawyers, courts, contracts all seen as either corrupt or irrelevant
5. No rule of law culture → Corruption becomes the de facto operating system

**Even Regime Allies Acknowledge This.** The Head of Iran's Judiciary has publicly had to defend "such fundamental concepts as the rule of law" — the fact that the regime's own chief judicial officer must argue for the concept's validity demonstrates how shallow its roots are (Boroumand Center).


### _L373) D14.4 Tax Evasion as Political Resistance
A critical development post-2019: tax evasion has evolved from economic survival behavior into a conscious form of political resistance.

- Former MP Hashemollah Falahatpisheh documented over 20 new taxes and fees imposed on citizens in a single parliamentary term
- Citizens increasingly use cash transactions and card-to-card transfers to avoid digital tax tracking
- Jeweler and bazaar strikes in multiple years (2023, 2024, 2025) have specifically targeted new tax enforcement measures
- The regime's response — ATM withdrawal limits of 200,000 tomans (~$3.60), unchanged since 2008 — forces citizens toward digital transactions where the state can monitor
- Meanwhile, regime-connected entities enjoy sweeping exemptions: EIKO, Astan Quds Razavi, Foundation of the Oppressed, IRGC conglomerates pay minimal or no tax. Mobarakeh Steel had an effective tax rate of 6%.

**The Political Logic:** Citizens see a system where they are taxed while the kleptocratic elite is exempt. This is not a perception — it is factually accurate. The rational response is to refuse participation. As one analysis noted: "Tax evasion has become a form of resistance against the state in Iran."

**The Transition Trap:** This creates a severe problem for any successor government. Citizens have learned — correctly, under this regime — that taxation is extraction without representation, and that compliance is for fools. Reversing this will require *visible* reciprocity (services delivered before taxes demanded) and *radical* transparency (no exemptions, especially for former elites), which are precisely the hardest things for a fragile new government to deliver.


### _L387) D14.5 The Generational Divide
The arbab mentality, taxation culture, and rule-of-law attitudes are not uniform across generations. A critical fault line exists:

**Older Generation (50+):**
- Lived experience of either Pahlavi paternalism or revolutionary welfare promises (or both)
- Stronger expectation that government *should* provide — subsidies, jobs, cheap goods
- More tolerant of a "strong leader" model (GAMAAN 2024: authoritarian rule finds more support among older, less-educated, rural populations)
- More likely to view taxes as theft by an illegitimate regime, but also more likely to accept the arbab model from a *legitimate* arbab
- "If only we had a good Shah" sentiment — wanting a benevolent patron, not a civic system

**Younger Generation (under 35, ~60% of population):**
- No memory of revolutionary promises being fulfilled; all they have known is decay
- Far more likely to demand rights-based governance (GAMAAN 2024: 37% prioritize individual freedoms and human rights as top political value; only 5% prioritize traditional/religious values)
- Higher rejection of authoritarian models, higher demand for civic accountability
- But: *no lived experience of what civic governance actually feels like*. Their aspirations are shaped by social media exposure to Western democracies, not by participatory experience
- 40% female and 25% male youth unemployment means many have never participated in the formal economy, never filed a tax return, never interacted with government as a *functioning* institution
- Risk: aspirational civic values without institutional literacy creates vulnerability to populist demagogues who promise shortcuts

**The 30-49 Cohort: The Disillusioned Middle.**
- Experienced reformist hopes (Khatami) and their crushing
- Many participated in 2009 Green Movement and were traumatized by its failure
- Most cynical about *all* political models — neither arbab paternalism nor civic democracy seems credible
- Highest rates of economic desperation (downward mobility from middle class)
- Most likely to have emigrated or to want to
- Lowest trust in any organized political force


### _L414) D14.6 Transition Implications: Building a Fiscal Contract From Zero
**The Core Challenge:** A post-transition government must simultaneously:
1. Maintain subsidy flows to prevent humanitarian catastrophe and political collapse
2. Build a tax base that currently barely exists (7% of GDP, 60%+ evasion/exemption)
3. Establish rule of law credibility among a population that has learned to view law as a tool of oppression
4. Shift mass psychology from "government as arbab" to "government as accountable service provider"
5. Do all of this while inheriting a broken economy, empty treasury, and massive expectations

**What This Means in Practice:**

**On Subsidies:** A new government *cannot* cut subsidies in its early years. This is not weakness — it is recognition that the social contract must be rebuilt sequentially. First, deliver visible, non-corrupt services. Then, gradually shift from universal subsidies to targeted support. The arbab mentality can be redirected: if people see the new government as "a good arbab," that is a transitional success, not a failure. The shift to civic citizenship is generational, not revolutionary.

**On Taxation:** The sequence matters enormously:
1. First: eliminate elite exemptions visibly and publicly (IRGC/bonyad/EIKO entities taxed at full rate)
2. Second: digitize and simplify the tax system (remove the 20-form bureaucratic maze that incentivizes bribery)
3. Third: earmark visible taxes to visible services (this road was paid by your district's VAT — signage, transparency)
4. Fourth: gradually expand the tax base with taxpayer rights (ombudsman, appeals, data protection)
5. Last: universal compliance enforcement — only after steps 1-4 have built minimum trust

Attempting step 5 first (enforcement) will be perceived as the new arbab being just like the old one: extracting from the weak while the strong are exempt. This is exactly what the Islamic Republic has done with its recent tax crackdowns, and it has deepened resistance rather than building compliance.

**On Rule of Law:** The zerangi culture cannot be "fixed" by legislation. It can only be displaced by *consistent institutional behavior* over years:
- Visible punishment of corrupt officials (especially from the *new* government) matters more than any legal reform
- Judicial independence — measurable, not declaratory — must be established as a first-year priority
- The single most powerful signal: a new government official who breaks the law and is actually prosecuted by the new system. This would be genuinely unprecedented in living Iranian memory and would do more for rule of law than any constitutional provision.

**On the Generational Strategy:** The younger generation's civic aspirations are a real asset but must be matched with institutional education and participatory structures. Transition planning should create immediate, visible opportunities for civic participation:
- Local/municipal governance as the primary laboratory for civic culture
- Youth civic service programs (non-military) tied to community development
- Transparent budgeting at local level — letting people *see* where money goes
- Jury-like citizen panels for local decisions — building the muscle of civic participation

**The Deepest Risk:** That the population, having been trained by both monarchy and theocracy to expect a patron, simply transfers the arbab expectation to whatever new authority emerges — and that the new authority, desperate for stability, accepts the role. This would create a "better managed" version of the same fundamental dynamic, postponing rather than resolving the governance crisis.

## D15) THE GEOGRAPHY OF LIVING CONDITIONS AND THE SMALL-CITY PROTEST SHIFT — NEW SECTION
### _L453) D15.1 The Demographic Inversion: Rural Collapse and Urban Marginalization
Iran's population geography has undergone a complete inversion within living memory. In 1976, 70% of Iranians lived in villages and 30% in cities. Today, approximately 76–77% are classified as urban and 23–24% rural (~21 million). [Fact — World Bank 2023 data]

But these headline numbers conceal a more catastrophic reality:

**Rural collapse by the numbers:**
- Of Iran's 69,000 documented villages, only 38,000 remain inhabited. **31,000 villages — 45% of all rural settlements — have been completely abandoned.** [Fact — Abdolkarim Hosseinzadeh, VP for Rural Development, November 2024]
- Over 10,000 remaining villages no longer have access to drinking water. [Fact — Fanack Water / multiple sources]
- Precipitation has declined approximately 40% since 2024 in some regions. Iran extracts 80–100% of its renewable freshwater annually — among the world's most water-stressed states. [Fact — World Bank, hydrologists]
- Iran has approximately 1 million wells, half illegal. 422 plains restricted or critical due to excessive groundwater extraction. 359 suffering land subsidence. [Fact — FAO, government data]
- In November 2025, state media introduced the term "water refugees" (āvāregān-e āb) to describe families fleeing drought-stricken interior provinces toward the Caspian coast. [Fact]
- Rural poverty reaches approximately 52% per the World Bank's October 2025 projection. In rural and marginalized urban areas, 40–50% live below the poverty line. [Fact — World Bank Poverty & Equity Brief]

**The "urban" category is misleading:**
- Many "cities" were administratively reclassified villages (threshold dropped from 10,000 to 3,500, now unclear). A village exceeding the census threshold becomes a "city" overnight with no change in infrastructure. [Fact — academic analysis]
- Iran's marginalized slum population (hashiyeh-neshin) has grown from approximately 600,000 thirty-five years ago to **20–25 million** by various official and semi-official estimates. Deputy VP Hosseinzadeh stated 20 million live in slums (2024). A former Ministry of Energy official put the figure at 25 million. Parliamentary estimates range from 11 million (formal informal settlements only) to 19 million (including worn-out urban areas). [Fact range — multiple official Iranian sources disagree on definition, but all show massive scale]
- The *cause* of marginalization has shifted. Pre-2010, it was primarily rural migrants seeking economic opportunity. Post-2015, it is increasingly urban residents who can no longer afford city rents, pushed to urban fringes — the "suburbanization of poverty." [Inference — Med, academic sources]

**What the real population distribution looks like:**

| Category | Estimated Population | Key Feature |
|----------|---------------------|-------------|
| **Tehran metro area** | ~15–16M | Macrocephalic capital, vastly overrepresented in media, politics, and analysis |
| **Major cities (Isfahan, Mashhad, Tabriz, Shiraz, etc.)** | ~20–25M | Where most Iran analysis focuses |
| **Secondary cities (200K–500K)** | ~10–12M | Provincial capitals, mixed development |
| **Small cities (50K–200K)** | ~8–12M | Often administratively neglected, poor services, economic periphery |
| **Small towns (<50K) and remaining villages** | ~25–30M | **The invisible Iran** — where ~30% of the population lives with 50%+ poverty, collapsing water, minimal healthcare, and almost no analytical attention |

This means **roughly a third of Iran's population lives in conditions that most Iran analyses, including our own project until now, substantially underweigh.** [Inference — High confidence]


### _L484) D15.2 Center-Periphery: The Structural Geography of Neglect
The pattern is not random. World Bank data, Iranian government studies, and academic analyses converge on the same map:

**Poorest, most deprived provinces** (highest poverty, highest unemployment, lowest services): Sistan-Baluchestan, Kurdistan, Kermanshah, Ilam, Kohgiluyeh and Boyer-Ahmad, Chaharmahal and Bakhtiari, Lorestan, Ardebil, Khuzestan. [Fact — World Bank, Iran Ministry of Labor 2023–2024]

**Wealthiest, most developed provinces:** Tehran, Alborz, Semnan, Isfahan, Fars. [Fact — same sources]

The correlation with ethnic geography is near-total. The poorest provinces are disproportionately non-Persian (Kurdish, Lur/Bakhtiari, Baloch, Arab) or geographically peripheral. The center-periphery economic divide maps onto the center-periphery ethnic divide.

This is not coincidental but structural: centralized governance concentrates capital, high-value services, and political authority in Tehran and the Persian-majority central plateau. Border provinces absorb security externalities (war with Iraq destroyed Kermanshah and Ilam infrastructure), suffer environmental degradation (Khuzestan water diversion, Lake Urmia collapse), and receive disproportionately less investment.

**Critical data point:** From 2010 to 2024, 97% of those executed on political charges in Iran were Kurdish, Baloch, or Arab. The Baloch, who comprise 2–6% of the population, accounted for 17% of narcotics-charge executions in 2024 and nearly half in 2021–2022. [Fact — Iran Human Rights organization, Oslo]


### _L498) D15.3 The Shifting Geography of Protest: A Phase Shift
The geographic spread of Iranian protests tells a story of progressive deepening:

| Protest Cycle | Cities/Towns | Geographic Character | Primary Demographics |
|---------------|-------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **1999 student protests** | 3 cities (Tehran, Babol, Tabriz) | Major universities only | Students, upper-middle class |
| **2009 Green Movement** | ~10 major cities | Tehran-centric, urban middle class | Reform-oriented, educated, Persian |
| **2017–2018 economic protests** | 80+ towns and cities | **First peripheral explosion.** Started in Mashhad, spread to small towns. | Low-income young men, provincial poor |
| **2019 fuel protests** | 100+ cities | Heavily peripheral, poor provinces | Working class, urban poor. 1,500 killed. |
| **2022 Woman Life Freedom** | 100+ cities, all 31 provinces | Ethnic periphery prominent — Kurdish and Baloch regions 40–50% of fatalities despite ~15% of population | Women, youth, Gen Z, ethnic minorities |
| **2025–2026 uprising** | 108+ cities in 8 days; 400+ eventually | **Zagros mountain heartland joins.** Former regime-base provinces (Ilam, Chaharmahal-Bakhtiari, Kohgiluyeh) now protest epicenters. Villages participate. | Bazaari (working poor layer), cross-class, cross-ethnic, cross-geographic. Broadest coalition yet. |

The pattern: each cycle is **wider, faster, more peripheral, and more existential** than the last. The intervals are shrinking: 8 years (2009–2017), 2 years (2017–2019), 3 years (2019–2022), 3 years (2022–2025). [Fact — compiled from multiple academic/analytical sources]

**What is genuinely new in 2025–2026:**

1. **Former regime-base provinces are protesting.** Ilam, Kohgiluyeh and Boyer-Ahmad, and Chaharmahal and Bakhtiari were long considered pillars of the Islamic Republic's social support — conservative, religious, economically dependent on state patronage. Their intense participation is not an extension of prior protest geography; it is its inversion. [Inference — High confidence, multiple analysts note this]

2. **Villagers are coming to protest.** In Qorveh (Kurdistan Province, pop. ~80,000), an eyewitness told CHRI: "Many had come from surrounding villages." In Chenaran (Khorasan, pop. ~55,000), "many of the young people living in this area are from villages, and I am certain they joined the protests." In Razgerdan village (pop. ~300 near Arak), "three or four people were killed, and 30 people were shot." [Fact — CHRI eyewitness testimony, Jan 2026]

3. **The violence moved to small cities first.** Critical Threats (ISW) noted on January 2: "The protests in Iran have become more violent since January 1, primarily in less populated and more rural areas... The concentration of the use of lethal force in more rural areas reflects Iranian security forces' *reduced capacity to suppress unrest and impose control in these areas.*" [Fact — Critical Threats/ISW, emphasis added]

4. **The arbab bargain has broken precisely where it was strongest.** These are populations most dependent on state subsidies, Relief Committee payments, and patronage networks. The fact that they are revolting despite maximal economic dependency confirms the D14 finding: the arbab model fails when the arbab cannot deliver. Subsidy elimination + rial collapse + inflation above 45% destroyed the last thread of the patronage contract. [Inference — High confidence]


### _L523) D15.4 What the IRGC Is Doing in Small Towns — And What It Means
The regime's response in small cities and villages differs qualitatively from its response in Tehran or Isfahan. The CHRI "Unreported Atrocities" report (January 30, 2026) provides the most detailed picture:

**Pattern of suppression in small cities:**

- **Shoot-to-kill orders issued simultaneously across the country** around 9 PM on January 8. Multiple eyewitnesses in different cities independently describe the same time. "Around 9 p.m., it was as if the order to kill had been issued." (Khorramabad, Andimeshk, multiple locations) [Fact — CHRI, cross-referenced eyewitness accounts]
- **IRGC and Basij as primary killing force**, not regular police. Snipers on mosque rooftops and government buildings. DShK heavy machine guns deployed on streets. Military-grade weapons against civilian populations. [Fact — CHRI, HRW/Amnesty]
- **Hospital infiltration:** Armed security forces stationed in hospitals to arrest wounded. In some cases, forced medical staff to treat only injured Basij/IRGC. In Kerman, Mehrgan Hospital cooperated with security forces. [Fact — CHRI, multiple sources]
- **Door-to-door raids as standard operating procedure** in small cities. Night raids designed to terrorize neighborhoods. In Andimeshk, families forced to strip to check for pellet wounds. In Khorramabad, "agents are going door to door searching for protesters." [Fact — CHRI eyewitness]
- **Collective punishment of families.** In Andimeshk, security forces went to the home of a killed protester, shot his father with a Kalashnikov, and arrested him along with two brothers. Families forced to pay 600 million tomans for bodies, or sign papers declaring the deceased was a Basij member (to inflate state casualty counts). [Fact — CHRI]
- **Unofficial detention facilities.** In Kerman, detainees transferred to warehouses and container units along Zangiabad Road under IRGC supervision — facilities "absent even from the IRGC's official detention lists." [Fact — CHRI]
- **Foreign militia deployment.** In Sanandaj (Kurdistan), eyewitnesses reported that "IRGC replaced at night by Iraqi Hashd al-Shaabi forces." The regime imported an estimated 800 foreign militia members. [Fact — CHRI, Wikipedia/reactions sources]

**The Chenar Village Case Study (February 16, 2026):**

Chenar is a village in Asadabad county, Hamedan province — western Iran. Previous videos showed residents carrying Lion and Sun flags and chanting anti-Khamenei slogans during the January protests.

On February 16, at 4:30 AM, security forces surrounded and raided the village with dozens of armored vehicles, minibuses, and vans. Four DShK heavy machine guns were deployed on rooftops of homes. Hundreds of residents arrested. Detainees paraded through the city in vehicles fitted with cage bars. Several villagers injured; detainees severely beaten. [Fact — Iran International, multiple sources]

**What the Chenar raid signifies:**

This is **collective punishment of an entire community**. Not targeted arrest of identified protest leaders — mass detention of a village population. The parading of prisoners in cage-bar vehicles is deliberate: a public performance of humiliation meant to deter surrounding communities. The DShK deployment on residential rooftops is a military occupation posture, not a law enforcement operation. [Inference — High confidence]


### _L547) D15.5 Why Small Cities Change the Calculus
The small-city dynamic introduces several factors that fundamentally alter the regime's strategic position:

**1. Anonymity collapses in both directions.** In Tehran, security forces can shoot anonymously into crowds. In Chenaran (pop. 55,000), "everyone knows each other. Everyone knows who was protesting and who was shooting." This creates a **permanent revenge ledger** that no amount of post-crackdown surveillance can erase. The CHRI eyewitness explicitly named the IRGC commander responsible: "Haj Habibollah (Heshmat) Derikvand, commander of IRGC Brigade 57 in Khorramabad. He personally ordered the attack." [Inference — High confidence, direct testimony]

**2. The Starlink gap cuts both ways.** Small cities have less Starlink access, which limits information flow to the outside world (the regime's intention). But it also means the scale of atrocities in small cities is *underdocumented*. CHRI explicitly noted that confirmed death tolls are based on major cities with Starlink; the violence in small cities may be substantially worse than what has reached international attention. [Fact — CHRI methodology note]

**3. The security force calculus is different.** Regime security forces face a capacity constraint: they cannot maintain heavy military presence in 400+ small cities simultaneously. The January 8–9 massacres required concentration of IRGC forces, leaving gaps elsewhere. But small cities are also easier to lock down post-massacre — fewer exits, more visible population, entire communities can be placed under effective martial law. The regime traded breadth for depth: it cannot prevent uprisings in small cities but can punish them afterward. [Inference — Med-High]

**4. Economic dependencies as hostage leverage.** In small cities, the state is often the dominant or sole employer. Government offices, schools, health clinics, military installations may account for most formal employment. The regime can threaten not just individual protesters but entire community livelihoods — exactly the leverage visible in the Chenar raid. [Inference — High confidence, structural]

**5. Tribal and clan dynamics amplify everything.** In Lur, Bakhtiari, Kurdish, and Baloch areas, family and clan networks mean that killing one person creates obligations across an extended kinship group. The Chenaran eyewitness warned: "Everyone who has lost a loved one, or was injured or arrested, will not easily forgive those responsible." This is not metaphorical in clan-structured societies — it is a social obligation. It means the regime's violence generates not just individual grievance but *collective debt* that persists across generations. [Inference — High confidence, anthropological pattern]


### _L561) D15.6 The Water-Protest Nexus
The overlap between water crisis geography and protest geography is striking and underexamined:

- **Khuzestan:** Water diversion to central provinces + Arab ethnic marginalization → 2021 "uprising of the thirsty" → 2025–2026 intense participation
- **Isfahan:** Gavkhouni wetland collapse + Zayanderud River drying → farmer protests April 2025 → 2025–2026 participation
- **Kurdistan:** Reservoir capacity at 38%, 25-point decline → general strikes January 2026
- **Lorestan/Ilam:** Zagros mountain groundwater depletion → agricultural collapse → among deadliest protest provinces

The Carnegie Endowment (November 2025) documented this nexus explicitly: water scarcity contributed to "several large protests" in Isfahan and Khuzestan in 2018, 2021, and 2025. These were "specifically triggered by water scarcity." The crisis is "disproportionately impacted" ethnic minority provinces "due in part to the state diverting the area's water supply to more privileged and prosperous provinces." [Fact — Carnegie]

**Transition implication:** Water crisis is not a separate issue from political crisis — it is the *same crisis in a different register*. Post-transition government inherits not just a broken political system but a **physically dying landscape**. Many abandoned villages will never be re-inhabited; aquifers are permanently depleted. Transition planning must include triage: which areas are salvageable, which require managed relocation, and how to prevent water conflict from becoming ethnic conflict. [Inference — High confidence]


### _L574) D15.7 Transition Implications: The Periphery Problem
**The analytical gap this section closes:** Most transition planning (and most Iran analysis generally) is Tehran-centric. It focuses on institutional architecture, elite negotiations, security force behavior, and constitutional design. These are necessary but insufficient. A third of the population lives in conditions where the basic question is not "what form of government?" but "will there be water?" and "will anyone come to arrest my son?"

**Key implications:**

**1. Decentralization is not optional — it is existential.** The center-periphery inequality that produced the protest geography is also the inequality that will produce separatist pressures in a transition. Kurdish, Baloch, and Arab regions have been disproportionately repressed AND disproportionately neglected. Federal or decentralized structures are not a concession to minority demands — they are the only way to prevent the periphery from seeking exit. [Inference — High confidence]

**2. Visible peripheral investment must precede political demands.** A new government asking peripheral provinces to "trust the process" after decades of economic looting and military repression will get the same response the Islamic Republic got: "show us the money first." Emergency water, healthcare, and infrastructure projects in the most deprived provinces — within the first 100 days — are a political necessity, not just a humanitarian one. [Inference — High confidence]

**3. The small-city revenge cycle must be addressed.** In communities where "everyone knows who was shooting," post-transition justice cannot be deferred indefinitely. But it also cannot be handled by the same central institutions that conducted the repression. Local truth-and-reconciliation mechanisms — adapted to tribal/clan social structures — may be the only path that avoids either impunity (which enrages victims) or mass retribution (which destabilizes). The South African TRC model is partially relevant; the Iraqi de-Baathification model is what to avoid. [Inference — Med-High]

**4. Rural/small-city populations are not merely victims — they are potential transition assets.** These communities have maintained social cohesion, mutual aid networks, and collective action capacity (demonstrated by the protests themselves) that atomized mega-city populations have lost. In places like Chenar, where a village collectively defies the IRGC despite knowing every member will be identified, the civic muscle for self-governance already exists — it just needs institutional channels. [Inference — Med]

## _L590) ANALYTICAL ASSESSMENT
**The single most important finding in this module:** The Islamic Republic's social base is not a loyal population held by ideology. It is a captive population held by economic dependency, fear, and absence of alternatives. The 8 million on the payroll, the 4.3 million on stipends, the 42% in informal employment — these are not constituencies to defeat. They are constituencies to absorb. Transition planning that treats "regime employees" as adversaries will reproduce the catastrophic de-Baathification error.

**The corruption finding confirms this:** Everyday corruption in Iran is survival behavior, not moral failure. Transition justice must clearly distinguish between systemic/elite corruption (which demands accountability) and survival corruption (which demands systems reform). The line between "corrupt official" and "underpaid teacher who tutors privately" is the line between justice and injustice in post-transition Iran.

**The arbab finding is the deepest layer:** The preceding findings are about *what* must be done. The D14 finding is about *what will resist it*. Iran has never — not under the Qajars, not under the Pahlavis, not under the Islamic Republic — operated under a genuine fiscal contract between citizen and state. The population has been conditioned across centuries of landlord rule and decades of oil rentierism to understand government as an *owner who distributes* rather than a *servant who is funded*. This is not a policy problem; it is a civilizational challenge. Any transition plan that assumes citizens will spontaneously pay taxes, obey laws, and engage in civic processes simply because a new government is more legitimate will fail. The fiscal contract must be *built*, sequentially and visibly, over years. The arbab mentality can be a transitional ally — a population that expects a patron can be directed toward constructive ends — but it must ultimately be transcended. This is the work of a generation, not a government.

**The D15 geography finding is the operational correction:** The arbab finding explains the cultural depth of the challenge. The geography finding reveals *where the challenge is most acute and least understood*. A third of the population — in small cities, degraded towns, and dying villages — has been systematically invisible in both regime governance and opposition planning. The 2025–2026 protest geography proved that these populations are politically activated, willing to risk death, and capable of collective action. But they are also the most vulnerable to collective punishment (Chenar), the most affected by environmental collapse (water refugees), and the least likely to benefit from any transition that remains Tehran-centric. The shift of protest epicenters to former regime-base provinces (Ilam, Chaharmahal-Bakhtiari, Lorestan) is not a tactical detail — it is evidence that the Islamic Republic has lost the periphery that once constituted its social base. No amount of IRGC violence can restore that lost contract. The question is whether a successor government will understand that rebuilding trust in the periphery — with water, with services, with justice, with genuine devolution of authority — is not one priority among many. It is *the* priority.
