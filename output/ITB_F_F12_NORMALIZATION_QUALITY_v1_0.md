## _L13) FRAMING: THE QUESTION THAT CHANGES EVERYTHING
F11 established *who* benefits from the current equilibrium. This module addresses a structurally distinct question:

**Among actors who would benefit from normalization, which ones care what KIND of normalization occurs?**

This is not a refinement of F11. It is a prerequisite for transition planning. The distinction determines:

- Whether a nuclear deal (without governance change) actually delivers the investment, capital, and technology Iran needs to reconstruct
- Whether the diaspora's resources deploy under partial normalization or only under governance change
- Whether IRGC economic power is weakened or enriched by sanctions relief
- Which scenario (1, 1A, 1B, or 5) external actors will actively support or undermine

Without this framework, transition planning misidentifies its resource base, mismodels the deal's economic effects, and designs conditionality for the wrong problem.

## _L30) SECTION 1: THE TWO-TYPE TAXONOMY
### _L32) Type A: Regime-Agnostic Normalization Beneficiaries
**Definition:** Actors whose benefit from normalization flows through geography, trade access, and commodity flows — independent of who controls Iranian institutions or whether the IRGC retains economic dominance.

**The structural logic:** Type A benefits are activated by **sanctions removal** alone. They do not require Western investment, rule of law, property rights protection, or IRGC divestiture. The transaction counterparty can be IRGC-controlled and Type A actors still gain.

**Key actors:**

| Actor | Benefit mechanism | Regime-agnostic because... | Confidence |
|-------|-----------------|---------------------------|------------|
| Pakistan | IP gas pipeline — $2B Iranian section built; Pakistan needs to complete 43-km section and receive gas | Counterparty is NIGC (not OFAC-designated IRGC affiliate); transaction is geography-determined | [Fact–Med] |
| India (Chabahar geography) | Shahid Beheshti terminal as Eurasian transit hub; Afghanistan/INSTC corridor | Geography is fixed; India needs access to the port, not ownership of Iranian governance | [Fact–Med] |
| Afghanistan / Central Asia | Chabahar as only non-Pakistan, non-China access to global markets | Landlocked geography forces transit dependency regardless of Iranian governance | [Fact–High] |
| Global shipping / insurance | Hormuz risk premium removal; strait stabilization reduces tanker rates, rerouting costs, insurance pricing | Hormuz is a waterway; normalization reduces risk regardless of Iranian government type | [Fact–High] |
| China (oil volume) | Iranian crude under reduced-discount conditions; expanded non-sanctioned trade | China's energy security interest is supply volume and price, not Iranian governance; IRGC control of NIOC is irrelevant to Chinese oil-for-goods exchange | [Fact–Med] |
| Humanitarian / food / medicine access | Iranian population can access imports | Sanctions relief enables trade regardless of distributional governance | [Fact–High] |
| Turkey (re-export arbitrage / transit) | Trade route revenues; Iranian consumer market access | Trade flows are geography and price-driven, not governance-driven | [Inference–Med] |
| Russia (sanctions-evasion infrastructure) | Preserve Iran as evasion partner and UNSC ally | Prefers Islamic Republic but economic relationship not blocked by IRGC control — already operates through it | [Inference–Med] |

**The Type A ceiling problem:** Type A normalization is achievable through a nuclear deal with no IRGC conditionality. But Type A normalization **does not** generate the investment, technology, and capital flows that Iran needs for economic reconstruction. It generates commodity trade and transit revenue. The economy rebuilds only partially. IRGC-controlled sectors (oil/gas/construction/telecom) capture the majority of the incremental income.


### _L55) Type B: Governance-Sensitive Normalization Beneficiaries
**Definition:** Actors whose benefit from normalization requires, as a legal or practical precondition, that IRGC economic control be reduced or that Iran achieve a functioning rule-of-law investment environment.

**The structural logic:** Type B benefits are blocked not by nuclear sanctions alone but by:
(a) Designation architecture — IRGC FTO/SDGT + entity-specific designations on NIOC, Khatam al-Anbiya, IRGC-linked banks that survive any nuclear deal
(b) Governance barriers — absence of property rights, contract enforcement, independent courts, and protection from IRGC predation that make sustained investment non-viable
(c) Post-Feb 2026: EU IRGC terrorist designation creates criminal liability for European companies transacting with IRGC-affiliated entities under EU law, independent of US sanctions status

**Key actors and their specific barriers:**


### _L66) B1: Western Oil Majors (TotalEnergies, Shell, BP, Eni, ExxonMobil)
**The prize:** South Pars / North Dome — world's largest gas field (~14.2 Tcm), containing the reserves that could make Iran a globally significant LNG exporter. Requires $35–50B in Western technology and capital for pressure restoration (the field is depleting; Qatar's Qatar Energy has invested heavily; Iran's side is underdeveloped). No Chinese company has the deepwater LNG technology Iran needs.

**The barrier:** NIOC (National Iranian Oil Company) was designated by OFAC in September 2012 as an "agent or affiliate" of the IRGC. Khatam al-Anbiya Construction Headquarters — the construction arm that would execute any large-scale field development — is explicitly and separately designated. A Western oil major signing a contract with NIOC or KAA faces:
- Criminal exposure under 18 USC 2339B (material support to FTO) — not removable by nuclear deal
- SDGT sanctions violation exposure under Treasury/OFAC authorities
- Board-level and shareholder liability under corporate governance

**Resolution requirement:** Either (a) IRGC removed from NIOC's governance structure and NIOC de-designated, or (b) Iranian governance transition sufficient to create a genuinely independent state oil company, or (c) specific Congressional-plus-executive action removing NIOC from IRGC-affiliate designation. Option (c) requires institutional reform evidence that the current regime cannot credibly provide.

**Transition relevance — CRITICAL:** South Pars development is the single largest economic asset in any Iranian transition scenario. The $35–50B investment required from Western majors is only accessible under governance change. This is the most important Type B finding in the entire framework. [Inference–High]


### _L79) B2: Western Banks and Financial Institutions
**The need:** SWIFT reconnection (achieved by nuclear deal) is necessary but insufficient. Correspondent banking — the dollar-clearing relationships that allow Iranian banks to operate internationally — requires due diligence that is blocked by IRGC penetration of the Iranian banking system.

**The barrier:** Multiple Iranian banks designated: Bank Mellat (UK, EU, US), Bank Melli (links to IRGC-affiliated entities), several others. Even non-designated Iranian banks face compliance exposure if their correspondent chains include IRGC-linked institutions. Post-February 2026 EU IRGC designation means European banks face criminal liability for transactions with IRGC-controlled financial entities under EU law.

**Practical effect:** A European bank establishing a correspondent relationship with an Iranian bank faces: (a) Know-Your-Customer requirements it cannot satisfy given IRGC opacity, (b) potential criminal liability if an Iranian correspondent bank turns out to have IRGC ownership, (c) reputational risk with US regulators (vital for dollar-clearing). SWIFT reconnection ≠ functional correspondent banking.

**Resolution requirement:** Functioning correspondent banking requires Iranian banking system reform sufficient to establish KYC-auditable institutions without IRGC ownership structures. This is a governance problem, not a sanctions problem. [Inference–High]


### _L89) B3: Western Technology and Consumer-Facing Companies
**The prize:** 85M-person consumer market with high education levels, significant tech adoption, and decades of pent-up demand.

**The barrier:** IRGC controls MCI (Iran's largest telecom, ~50M subscribers). IRGC blocked the Divar platform IPO in 2025 — documented case of IRGC extracting equity from a successful private tech company as the price of operating. WaPo (August 2025) documents IRGC pressure on tech founders as systematic, not episodic.

A foreign tech company entering Iran under IRGC-dominant governance faces: (a) IRGC as an uninvited equity partner via extortion, (b) surveillance infrastructure owned by designated terrorist entity, (c) data sovereignty exposure, (d) compliance violation if IRGC forces share of revenue. The market exists only if property rights are protected. Under current conditions, entering the Iranian market is not a business decision — it is a structured extraction.

**Resolution requirement:** IRGC divestiture from MCI and telecom sector; functioning commercial law. [Inference–High]


### _L99) B4: Iranian Diaspora Capital (The Most Underanalyzed Type B Actor)
**The scale:** The Iranian diaspora is estimated at 5–8 million globally (concentrated in US, Canada, Germany, UK, UAE, Australia, Israel). Accumulated wealth is not formally tracked but the professional profile (disproportionately engineers, physicians, entrepreneurs, academics, finance professionals) and residence in high-income countries suggests accumulated capital of $1–3T, with significant entrepreneurial capital accessible for rapid deployment on short investment horizons.

**This is not a marginal figure.** South Korea's economic reconstruction was significantly accelerated by Korean-American capital repatriation in the 1960s–1970s. Taiwan's semiconductor industry was seeded by returning diaspora engineers. The diaspora capital-return dynamic is one of the most powerful reconstruction accelerants available to any transition.

**The barrier — exclusively governance:**
- Property rights: Under IRGC-dominant governance, any asset repatriated to Iran faces expropriation risk through no-bid contract displacement, regulatory extortion, or direct seizure. Iranian-Americans who have invested in Iran since 1979 have documented patterns of asset extraction.
- Banking: Where does diaspora capital land? IRGC-penetrated banking system with OFAC-designated banks is not usable for diaspora investors with US/EU residency.
- Personal security: Returning Iranians with political profiles face detention, forced intelligence cooperation, or travel bans. 65% of Iran's engineering and science graduates plan to emigrate within 2 years of completing degrees (ISNA 2023). Brain drain reversal requires personal security guarantees that only governance change can provide.
- Legal framework: No functioning commercial dispute resolution under IRGC-dominant judiciary (see ITB-D16 for judiciary reconstruction analysis).

**Diaspora capital is exclusively Type B.** It does not deploy under hollow deal. It deploys only under genuine governance transition. This means transition planning that relies on diaspora capital as a reconstruction resource must treat governance change as a precondition, not an aspiration.

**The political dimension:** Diaspora is not just a capital source — it is an active political actor in US, Canadian, European, and German domestic politics. The February 14 Global Day of Action (1M+ participants across 30 countries) demonstrated political mobilization capacity. Diaspora political action is oriented toward governance change, not deal-making. When Scenario 1B (Pinochet Pivot) is analyzed against diaspora political response, diaspora becomes an active variable in whether the deal survives politically in Washington and Brussels.

**Cross-reference:** ITB-G §G6 (transition relevance) must be read with this analysis. Current G6 does not encode the governance precondition. See Integration Spec for required update. [Inference–High]


### _L117) B5: Iran's Private Sector and Entrepreneurial Class
**The paradox:** The Iranian entrepreneurs most capable of rebuilding a post-sanctions economy are the population most harmed by IRGC economic dominance. IRGC is not a neutral actor in the Iranian economy — it is an active competitor, extractor, and suppressor of private enterprise.

**The evidence:** 
- Divar (Iran's largest classifieds platform) was forced to cancel its IPO in 2025 after IRGC pressure; founders faced surveillance and coercion (WaPo, August 6, 2025)
- 800+ Khatam al-Anbiya subsidiaries receive no-bid contracts in sectors where independent contractors could compete
- Banking credit allocation is directed toward IRGC-linked entities, starving private sector capital access
- "Privatization" since 2005 transferred state assets to IRGC-linked holding companies, not competitive private bidders

A nuclear deal that preserves IRGC economic dominance does not unlock private sector growth. It gives the IRGC access to more resources to extract from the private sector. Lifting sanctions increases the value of economic assets; IRGC will move to capture those gains. The private sector requires IRGC economic divestiture. [Inference–High]


### _L129) B6: European Union (Post-February 2026 Designation)
**The date:** EU designated IRGC as terrorist organization in late January/early February 2026. This is analytically significant beyond symbolism.

**The legal effect:** EU companies in sectors where IRGC has ownership, control, or facilitation links face criminal liability under EU counterterrorism law — not merely sanctions fines. The criminal standard is materially different from civil/regulatory sanctions: it triggers personal liability for corporate officers, not just company fines.

**The economic scope:** IRGC controls 20-40% of Iran's formal economy. The sectors of highest European investment interest — oil/gas, construction, infrastructure, banking — are the sectors of deepest IRGC penetration. European commercial re-engagement with Iran post-nuclear-deal is now legally blocked in the most economically significant sectors by the IRGC designation, under EU law that survives any deal.

**The political effect:** By designating IRGC as terrorist organization, the EU has created a legal barrier to hollow-deal normalization that cannot be reversed without a formal EU Council decision. Any EU government that wants to enable European investment in Iran post-deal must publicly vote to de-designate an organization that murdered Iranian protesters — politically impossible in the current European climate.

**The implication for transition planning:** EU re-engagement as a major investment source (European reconstruction funds, development finance, private capital) requires either IRGC de-designation (politically impossible under current conditions) or IRGC economic divestiture sufficient to separate the investable Iranian economy from the designated entity. This is a structural governance condition, not a sanctions condition.

## _L143) SECTION 2: THE NORMALIZATION QUALITY LADDER
The following matrix maps outcomes against who benefits and at what magnitude. This is the core analytical tool for evaluating any proposed deal against transition planning goals.

| Outcome | Type A benefit | Type B benefit | IRGC net position | Diaspora capital | South Pars access (Western) | Qatar competitive risk |
|---------|---------------|---------------|-------------------|-----------------|---------------------------|----------------------|
| **Status quo (sanctions)** | None | None | Current rents; sanctions evasion premium | Absent | Blocked | Protected |
| **Hollow deal** (nuclear only, IRGC structure intact) | High | Low | **Enriched** — sanctions relief flows through IRGC-controlled channels; IRGC gains without divestiture | Absent — governance barrier unchanged | Blocked — NIOC designation, KAA designation survive | Medium — Chinese companies develop S. Pars under reduced sanctions environment |
| **IRGC-dominant normalization** (full sanctions off, IRGC intact) | High | Low–Medium | **Maximally enriched** | Absent–Low | Blocked | High — Chinese S. Pars acceleration |
| **Genuine transition** (IRGC economically curtailed, governance change) | High | High | Reduced/eliminated | **Full deployment** | Open | **Very high** — Western majors enter; Qatar's structural advantage eliminated |
| **Collapse (Scenario 1–2 chaos)** | Negative | Negative | Eliminated but chaotically | Deferred; returns post-stabilization | Deferred | Low — instability reduces Iranian production |

**Critical finding:** The most likely diplomatic outcome — a hollow deal — is the **worst outcome for transition planning** among the positive scenarios. It:
- Enriches the IRGC without requiring divestiture
- Keeps Western investment, diaspora capital, and Type B actors locked out
- Allows Chinese companies (not subject to US/EU IRGC designation barriers) to develop South Pars — handing the reconstruction-financing asset to China
- Reduces external pressure on the regime at the moment when internal pressure (mourning cycle, economic collapse) is highest
- Validates Scenario 1B (Pinochet Pivot) as the analytical baseline

**The China vacuum irony:** US maximum pressure forces India out of Chabahar. A hollow deal reduces pressure while leaving IRGC designation barriers intact for Western firms. Result: Iran turns to China for both oil sector development and port operation — the exact strategic assets the US policy architecture was designed to prevent China from acquiring. US policy is self-defeating on its own stated strategic terms. [Inference–High]

## _L166) SECTION 3: TRANSITION PLANNING IMPLICATIONS BY PILLAR
### _L168) Implication for Pillar A (Regime Architecture / IRGC)
The Type B analysis reframes the IRGC transition problem. The current framing (Trap 1: IRGC Economic Bootstrapping Problem) treats IRGC economic divestiture as a simultaneity trap — you can't reform the economy without dismantling IRGC economic control, but dismantling it collapses employment.

The Type B framework adds a **positive incentive structure** that was missing from Trap 1's resolution path:

The actors who most need IRGC economic divestiture (Western oil majors, Western banks, EU companies, diaspora capital) represent **$50–100B+ in prospective investment** that is specifically conditional on IRGC divestiture. This is not speculative future investment — it is documented investment interest blocked by a specific, addressable legal/governance barrier.

**The IRGC transition offer structure:** A genuine transition framework can offer IRGC pragmatists an explicit trade: controlled economic divestiture → Western investment unlocked → IRGC-linked entities participate as minority shareholders in joint ventures with Western majors → economic gains for IRGC affiliates in a normalized environment exceed rents from IRGC monopoly in a sanctioned environment. This trade is only credible if the IRGC understands that without divestiture, the Western investment (and the reconstruction income) never arrives — only Chinese investment on China's terms does.

**Scenario 5 reframing:** Scenario 5 (Managed Transition) currently describes IRGC pragmatists trading political power for economic preservation. The Type B analysis clarifies this: they would be trading *monopoly control* for *minority participation in a much larger pie*. The economic calculation favors the trade — but only if the alternative (Chinese-only development under continued isolation) is made visible as the counterfactual. [Inference–High]


### _L180) Implication for Pillar C (Economy)
Current ITB-C framing treats sanctions relief as the primary economic intervention. The Type B analysis requires adding:

**Sanctions relief without IRGC divestiture = partial economic recovery, IRGC-captured.** The oil sector, construction sector, telecom sector, and banking sector — the commanding heights of the Iranian economy — remain under IRGC control and blocked to Western capital regardless of nuclear deal status. Only non-IRGC sectors (agriculture, consumer goods, some services) would see genuine private sector development under hollow normalization.

The economic reconstruction scale difference: Type A normalization (hollow deal) unlocks perhaps $10–20B/year in incremental oil revenue and trade. Type B normalization (governance change) unlocks $50–100B+ in prospective investment over 5 years plus diaspora capital plus technology transfer. The order of magnitude difference is the difference between stabilization and reconstruction. [Inference–Med]


### _L188) Implication for Pillar G (Diaspora)
Current ITB-G §G6 lists diaspora as a transition resource without encoding the governance precondition. This is analytically incomplete.

**Required addition to G6:** Diaspora capital and professional repatriation are exclusively Type B resources. They activate only under governance change — specifically: functioning property rights, IRGC divestiture from banking (deposit destination), personal security guarantees (no detention of returning diaspora with political profiles), and judicial system capable of contract enforcement.

**The February 14 signal:** The diaspora's political mobilization (1M+ globally) was oriented toward regime change, not deal-making. This is not an accident of messaging — it reflects a structural understanding that Type B benefits require governance change. The diaspora has, intuitively, solved the normalization quality problem. Their political action is correctly calibrated to their economic interests.

**Diaspora political agency under Scenario 1B (Pinochet Pivot):** Under hollow deal, diaspora becomes an active constraint on deal durability in Western capitals. Iranian-Americans in Los Angeles, Toronto Iranians, Munich Iranians — these communities have demonstrated the capacity to maintain political pressure in US, Canadian, and German domestic politics. A deal that betrays the January 2026 massacre victims will face sustained diaspora political opposition in all three countries. This is a transition planning resource: diaspora pressure is a mechanism for preventing hollow deal consolidation. [Inference–Med]


### _L198) Implication for Pillar F (International)
Every ITB-F actor assessment must now be evaluated against the Type A/B distinction. Key reclassifications and additions:

**Qatar:** Type A (re-export arbitrage, port revenues) + governance-agnostic on Iranian supply side. But *competitive* risk is governance-sensitive: Western majors can only develop South Pars under Type B normalization. Qatar's worst outcome is Type B normalization. Qatar is therefore an active opponent of governance change, not just an equilibrium beneficiary. This subtlety was absent from the F11 analysis. [Inference–High]

**UAE:** Similar to Qatar for re-export/financial arbitrage (Type A). But UAE has begun repositioning toward Western investment norms. UAE Type B risk: if Iran achieves genuine transition with functioning property rights and Western investment, UAE loses its role as the primary regional hub for trade that "happens to reach" Iran via third parties. UAE's long-run Type B interest is complex — it depends on whether UAE sees Iran as a competitor or a partner in a normalized regional economy.

**Iraq/PMF:** Type A — economic ties (energy trade, Shia political networks) operate regardless of Iranian governance type. PMF-Iran relationship is actually weakened under Iranian governance transition (new Iranian government less likely to fund PMF; Iraqi political dynamics shift). Iraq is a Type A actor with a governance-change downside. [Inference–Med]

**Oman:** Confirmed Tier 2 (mediation industry) from F11 addendum. Oman's optimal outcome under this framework: indefinite negotiation hovering between hollow deal and no deal — the re-export arbitrage ($803M documented) continues, mediation function is preserved, normalization never reaches Type B quality. Oman structurally opposes Type B normalization. [Inference–Med]

**China:** Classic Type A. Governance-agnostic energy security. IRGC economic dominance is not a barrier for China — it is a feature. Chinese companies operate through state-to-state channels that bypass the IRGC designation barriers. Under hollow normalization, China captures South Pars development. Under genuine transition, Western majors displace China from Iran's most valuable economic assets. China is therefore an active opponent of Type B normalization (governance change) and a supporter of Type A normalization (hollow deal). [Inference–High]

## _L214) SECTION 4: THE HOLLOW DEAL AS TRANSITION PLANNING PROBLEM
### 4.1) Why a Hollow Deal Is the Most Dangerous Scenario for Transition
Scenario 1B (Pinochet Pivot) in ISA-SCENARIOS describes a deal that provides regime survival and repression capacity. The Type B analysis adds a structural economic dimension that makes Scenario 1B worse than previously modeled:

**Under a hollow deal:**
1. IRGC receives incremental oil revenue from sanctions relief — estimated $10–20B/year additional income flowing through IRGC-controlled NIOC. This is not reconstruction investment — it is regime hardening revenue.
2. Western oil majors cannot enter (NIOC designation survives deal). Chinese companies can expand under reduced secondary sanctions pressure. South Pars development accelerates under Chinese terms — Chinese companies get preferred access; IRGC captures Chinese investment flows.
3. Diaspora capital does not repatriate. Brain drain continues or accelerates (educated Iranians see hollow deal as regime consolidation; emigration is the rational response).
4. Private sector entrepreneurs face intensified IRGC extraction: sanctions relief increases the value of economic assets; IRGC moves to capture the incremental value. Divar-type predation expands.
5. EU companies remain legally blocked (IRGC designation). The manufacturing and technology investment Iran needs for non-oil economic diversification does not arrive.
6. Iran 5 years after hollow deal: more oil revenue, captured by IRGC; no Western technology transfer; no diaspora return; private sector further hollowed; reconstruction further delayed.

**This is not Scenario 1 (deal + regime preservation). This is Scenario 1B (deal + IRGC enrichment + transition conditions worsened).** The distinction must be encoded in transition planning.


### 4.2) Conditionality as the Intervention Point
The Type B analysis directly identifies the conditionality requirements that separate hollow deal from genuine transition pathway:

**Type B-unlocking conditionality (what must be in any deal to enable reconstruction):**
1. **NIOC governance restructuring** — IRGC commanders removed from NIOC board; OFAC de-designation contingent on verified restructuring. This unlocks Western oil major investment.
2. **Khatam al-Anbiya competition requirement** — Construction contracts in IRGC-related sectors must be opened to competitive bidding with international observers. Specific KAA-designated entity activities suspended. This unlocks Western construction and engineering investment.
3. **Banking system transparency** — FATF compliance milestones with verified IRGC divestiture from bank ownership as condition for correspondent banking restoration. This unlocks Western financial sector.
4. **Commercial law framework** — Independent commercial courts (not Haqqani-pipeline judiciary; see ITB-D16) as condition for diaspora capital investment guarantees. This unlocks diaspora capital.
5. **Telecoms sector** — MCI governance reform (IRGC divestiture from majority ownership) as condition for Western tech market access. This unlocks consumer market.

These five conditionality requirements are specific, verifiable, and directly map to the five Type B actor categories. They are not abstract human rights demands — they are structural economic conditions with clear verification mechanisms. This makes them more negotiable than human rights conditionality because they are commercially legible to the US/EU business community.

**The argument for conditionality framing:** "If IRGC doesn't divest from NIOC, TotalEnergies can't enter, and Iran's gas field produces at 40% capacity for China at 20% discount while Qatar captures the global LNG market." This is an economic argument, not a moral argument. It may be more effective in the current US negotiating environment (Witkoff/Kushner transactional framework) than human rights conditionality. [Inference–Med]

## _L247) SECTION 5: THE ENCIRCLEMENT COUPLING (NEW — from Session 12)
**Finding:** The TRIPP corridor (August 8, 2025 US-Armenia-Azerbaijan agreement) + USS Abraham Lincoln carrier group + Israeli military degradation of IRGC infrastructure creates an observable three-front encirclement that provides A10 hardliners (Mirbagheri/Paydari/MASAF) with empirical evidence for their resistance narrative.

**Mechanism:** The normalization quality framework intersects with A10 eschatological faction dynamics in a specific way:

1. Any Type B normalization conditions (IRGC divestiture) are framed by A10 faction as capitulation under encirclement — "they are demanding we disarm while their forces sit on our borders"
2. The encirclement evidence is factually observable: TRIPP corridor on northern border, US carrier group in Arabian Sea, Israeli military degradation ongoing
3. Iranian pragmatists cannot factually refute the encirclement — they can only argue it's manageable. A10 hardliners can argue it proves that any deal is a trap.
4. This creates an A10 veto mechanism against precisely the conditionality (IRGC divestiture) that would enable Type B normalization

**Implication:** External pressure (TRIPP, military buildup) and internal spoiler capacity (A10) are coupled. Maximizing external military pressure while demanding IRGC divestiture as deal conditionality is self-defeating — the military pressure feeds the A10 faction's resistance narrative and makes pragmatist acceptance of conditionality politically impossible within the regime.

**Transition planning tension:** The conditions that would unlock Type B normalization (IRGC divestiture conditionality) are most achievable when external military pressure is reduced to a level where pragmatists can accept conditions without appearing to capitulate under encirclement. But reducing military pressure reduces leverage for demanding conditionality. This is a genuine strategic dilemma without a clean resolution. The sequencing matters: de-escalation first → negotiate conditionality → snap-back if violated, is more likely to produce Type B conditions than: maximum pressure → demand IRGC divestiture → A10 vetoes. [Inference–Med, TRIPP evidence base Fact–High]
