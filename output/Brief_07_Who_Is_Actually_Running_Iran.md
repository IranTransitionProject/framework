# Who Is Actually Running Iran?

## The Larijani Revelation and Why the Nuclear Talks Have the Wrong Man at the Table

**By Hooman Mehr | [hooman@mac.com](mailto:hooman@mac.com) | Brief \#7 | [Iran Transition Project](https://hmehr.substack.com/p/iran-the-convergence-briefs) | February 2026**

## The Phone Call That Explains Everything

When Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi wanted to contact Steve Witkoff after the second round of Geneva talks, he did not call President Pezeshkian for authorization. Pezeshkian told him to call Ali Larijani.

This detail, reported by the New York Times on February 22, 2026, citing six senior Iranian officials including three IRGC members, is not a palace intrigue anecdote. It is the single most important structural finding about Iran's nuclear negotiations since they resumed \-- because it confirms, with named sourcing at the highest level, that the person making decisions for Iran is not at the negotiating table. The person at the table cannot make decisions without calling someone who is not there. And the person who is not there answers to someone who has already rejected the deal's basic terms.

That chain \-- Araghchi to Larijani to Khamenei \-- is the actual decision architecture. Geneva is negotiating with the last link. The first link has already said no.

## Who Larijani Is

Ali Larijani is 67 years old. He is the Secretary of the Supreme National Security Council. He is a former IRGC commander. He is a former Speaker of Parliament. He ran for president twice and was disqualified by the Guardian Council once \-- an act that tells you both that he has ambitions and that the system's gatekeepers consider him insufficiently loyal. He is, by every account, a pragmatic institutional operator: not a reformist, not a theologian, but a man who understands every lever in the Islamic Republic's machinery because he has personally pulled most of them.

Since early January 2026, Khamenei has delegated day-to-day governance of Iran to Larijani. President Pezeshkian \-- a cardiac surgeon elected on a reformist platform \-- publicly acknowledges his own irrelevance. "I'm a doctor, not a politician," he has told associates. His role in the current crisis is to sign what Larijani decides and appear on camera when a presidential face is needed.

Larijani is liaising with Russia, Qatar, and Oman. Larijani is overseeing the nuclear negotiations. Larijani was in charge of the security response to the January protests \-- the operation that killed thousands. And Larijani has ordered a four-layer succession plan for every key position that Khamenei appoints, a directive that only makes sense if the system is preparing for Khamenei's removal from power \-- by death, incapacitation, or design.

This is not a temporary arrangement. It is a structural reorganization of the Islamic Republic's command architecture under crisis conditions. The elected president is ceremonial. The appointed security chief is governing. And the Supreme Leader, 86 and diminished, has effectively delegated his constitutional authority to a man who holds no constitutional office that grants it.

## What This Means for Geneva

The implications for the Thursday talks in Geneva are immediate and concrete.

**The authorization chain is a bottleneck, not a delegation.** Araghchi cannot accept, reject, or modify any proposal without Larijani's approval. Larijani cannot approve anything that Khamenei has not sanctioned. This means that every American proposal transits a three-person chain before receiving a response. Each link in that chain has different incentives: Araghchi wants a deal (his career depends on diplomatic success), Larijani wants regime survival (his power depends on managing the crisis), and Khamenei wants theological consistency (his legacy depends on not surrendering to the Great Satan). A proposal that satisfies one link fails at the next. This is not a negotiating tactic. It is a structural incapacity to make binding commitments at the speed Geneva requires.

**The enrichment contradiction is a symptom, not a miscommunication.** Araghchi told MSNBC on February 21 that "the US side has not asked for zero enrichment." The same day, Axios reported that Witkoff and Kushner explicitly told Araghchi that Trump's position is zero enrichment, while simultaneously signaling willingness to consider "token enrichment." Both statements can be true \-- Araghchi is reporting what he has been authorized to report, which is different from what was said. The gap between what each side says was said is not diplomatic ambiguity. It is the observable signature of an authorization chain where the man at the table cannot acknowledge what was communicated because acknowledging it would require authorization he does not have.

**The proposal Iran is preparing may not survive its own approval process.** Araghchi told CBS on February 22 that Iran's written proposal is still being finalized and that "political leadership in Tehran has yet to sign off." In the Larijani architecture, "political leadership" does not mean Pezeshkian. It means Larijani \-- and ultimately Khamenei. A proposal ambitious enough to interest Washington may be too ambitious for Khamenei to authorize. A proposal conservative enough for Khamenei to authorize may be too conservative for Washington to accept. Araghchi is trapped between two principals with incompatible requirements, and neither of them is at the table to resolve the gap in real time.

## The Deeper Problem: Two Decision-Makers Who Cannot Both Say Yes

Brief \#5 in this series identified the "wrong interlocutor" problem: Washington negotiates with the pragmatist wing, which controls roughly 15-25% of institutional power, while the deal's survival depends on overcoming the other 45-65% \-- theological opponents who view any agreement as betrayal, and institutional resisters whose economic interests depend on non-compliance.

The Larijani revelation sharpens this finding. It is not merely that Washington is negotiating with the wrong faction. It is that Washington is negotiating with an individual who must seek permission from a chain of command that terminates in a man who has already publicly rejected the deal's core terms. Khamenei stated on February 17 that he would not accept Trump's negotiation conditions. This was not a negotiating position. It was a theological declaration from a leader whose remaining political capital depends on not appearing to submit.

Larijani himself is not the problem \-- he may genuinely want a deal. He is an institutional pragmatist who has opposed the eschatological Paydari faction since their political split in 2011\. He understands that a military confrontation threatens the system he has spent his career building. But Larijani's pragmatism is constrained by the same structural reality the previous briefs have documented: the Supreme Leader's theological veto, the IRGC's economic resistance to any reform that touches their empire, and the Paydari faction's capacity to frame any concession as apostasy.

The result is a negotiation where the American side has a decision-maker (Trump) who can change terms in real time, and the Iranian side has a representative (Araghchi) who must relay proposals through a chain that cannot change terms at all \-- because each change requires theological clearance from a Supreme Leader who has staked his legacy on refusal.

## The War Preparation Signal

The Larijani revelation contains a second finding that has received less attention than it deserves.

The four-layer succession directive \-- ordering backup plans for every key position Khamenei appoints \-- is not contingency planning in the normal sense. It is institutional preparation for a specific scenario: Khamenei's removal from the decision chain, whether by death, incapacitation, or a strike that targets regime leadership.

Read alongside the other signals from the same reporting \-- ballistic missile launchers deployed on western borders and southern Gulf shores, a new Defense Council created under Ali Shamkhani, all armed forces at their highest readiness level \-- the succession directive indicates that Tehran is not bluffing about war preparation. The regime is genuinely preparing for a strike it considers likely. Larijani is simultaneously managing negotiations designed to prevent the strike and institutional preparations designed to survive it.

This dual posture \-- negotiate and prepare \-- is itself rational. But it produces a specific problem for the negotiations: the same institutional energy devoted to war preparation competes with the institutional energy needed for diplomatic flexibility. You cannot simultaneously harden your military posture and soften your negotiating position. The officials managing missile deployments are not the ones drafting compromise proposals. The system is doing both, under one man's coordination, and neither task is getting the attention it requires.

## What Happens Thursday

Geneva Round 3 convenes Thursday, February 26 \-- confirmed by Oman's Foreign Minister. Iran's written proposal is expected by Tuesday. Trump's deadline window opens approximately March 1-6. Witkoff told Fox News on Saturday that Trump is "curious" why Iran has not "capitulated." The largest US military force assembled since the 2003 Iraq invasion is in theater.

The structural analysis in this brief suggests that Thursday's meeting faces a specific failure mode that is not about the content of Iran's proposal. It is about the authorization architecture behind it. Even if Araghchi presents a proposal that appears to move toward Washington's position, the question that must be asked is: who authorized this, and can they deliver implementation against the 45-65% of institutional power that will resist it?

If the answer is "Larijani authorized it, but Khamenei's approval is conditional and reversible" \-- then the proposal is a diplomatic performance, not a binding commitment. It may buy time. It will not buy a deal.

The previous six briefs in this series have documented why: the theological deception doctrine that decouples presentation from intent, the parallel society that cannot be reformed by external agreement, the structural differences that make Iran's transition unlike any precedent, the spoiler faction that has killed every deal for nineteen years, the Pinochet mechanism that ensures any hollow deal enriches the regime's worst elements, and the military posture that gives this analysis a timeline measured in days.

Brief \#7 adds the capstone: the man who can say yes is not at the table. The man at the table cannot say yes without asking someone who has already said no.
