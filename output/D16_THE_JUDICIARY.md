# PILLAR D: THE JUDICIARY: 47 YEARS OF INSTITUTIONAL DESTRUCTION

**Last verified:** 2026-02-18 | **Confidence:** [Med-High]

---

**BLUF:** Iran’s judiciary is not merely dysfunctional — it has been systematically rebuilt over 47 years as a theological enforcement instrument answerable solely to the Supreme Leader. The modern secular judiciary created by Ali-Akbar Davar in 1926–1929 was progressively dismantled after 1979: secular judges purged and replaced with seminary-educated clerics, female judges banned, the Bar Association shut down, revolutionary courts established outside constitutional authority, and in 1994 the entire court structure was dissolved and rebuilt as single-judge sharia courts. The result is the institution most thoroughly captured by the clerical establishment — more so even than the military, which retains secular professional elements. A successor government inherits not a reformable judiciary but a legal vacuum filled by theocratic patronage. Rebuilding it is a prerequisite for every other transition task — rule of law, property rights, foreign investment, transitional justice — and also the single hardest institution to reconstruct.


---

### D16.1) Historical Context: What Was Destroyed
Iran had a functioning modern judiciary before the Islamic Republic. Understanding what existed is essential for understanding the scale of reconstruction required.

- **1926–1929:** Ali-Akbar Davar, with the mandate of Reza Shah and aided by French judicial experts, dissolved the entire existing judiciary and rebuilt it from scratch — new organizations, new court layers, new laws, new procedures, new judicial lexicon. By 1929, Iran had a fully operational modern secular judiciary modeled on the French civil law system. [Fact — High]
- **1936:** Religious courts formally abolished; their remaining jurisdiction over family and inheritance absorbed into secular system. [Fact — Med]
- **Pre-1979 state:** Iran’s judiciary, while subject to political interference (especially by SAVAK in security cases), operated with written law codes, multi-judge panels, formal appeals processes, a trained legal profession, and an independent Bar Association. Female judges served on the bench. The Bar Association’s independence was enshrined in law (1953 Bill of Independence signed by Mossadegh). [Fact — Med]

**What was lost:** A 50-year institutional tradition of secular legal practice, a trained judiciary familiar with codified law, a functional appeals system, a body of precedent, female judges, and an independent bar. None of these exist today in recognizable form.

### D16.2) The Three Phases of Judicial Destruction
**Phase 1 (1979–1982): Revolutionary Purge**

- Between 1979 and 1982, the entire pre-revolutionary judiciary was purged. Secular-trained jurists replaced with seminary-educated clerics who had “very little if any education beyond elementary school and basic Arabic language training at the seminary.” [Fact — Iran International, Encyclopaedia Iranica]
- All female judges dismissed or given clerical (non-judicial) functions within the Ministry of Justice. [Fact — Encyclopaedia Iranica]
- Bar Association shut down (1980 Law on Purging the Bar Association). Board of directors arrested and imprisoned. Lawyers accused of “subversive ideas” had licenses revoked. [Fact — Wikipedia/Bar Association history]
- Revolutionary Tribunals established by Khomeini’s direct edict — not by constitutional authority. Intended as temporary; still operational 47 years later. Presiding judges were clerics appointed by Khomeini personally. [Fact — High]
- Revolutionary courts operated as kangaroo courts: trials lasting minutes, no defense attorneys (dismissed as “Western absurdity”), conviction on basis of “popular repute,” and the novel charge of mofsed-e-filarz (“spreading corruption on earth”). [Fact — Med, multiple sources]
- Since there were insufficient qualified clergy to fill all judicial positions, some former secular judges who demonstrated Islamic law expertise and underwent religious training were permitted to remain. Nevertheless, the Islamization forced roughly half of all former civil court judges out. A special religious law school was established in Qom in 1980 to train replacement judges. [Fact — Encyclopaedia Iranica, Globalex]

**Phase 2 (1989–1994): Structural Capture**

- 1989 constitutional amendments abolished the Supreme Judicial Council (five-member body) and replaced it with a single Head of the Judiciary appointed directly by the Supreme Leader for renewable five-year terms. This concentrated total judicial authority in one person answerable to one person. [Fact — Boroumand Center, IHRDC]
- Head of Judiciary given power to appoint, promote, and dismiss judges — with “interests of the society” (undefined) as grounds for removal. Guardian Council affirmed this interpretation, giving the Supreme Leader’s appointee a free hand to shape the entire judiciary’s personnel. [Fact — Boroumand Center]
- 1987: Special Clerical Court established — operates independently of the regular judiciary, accountable only to the Supreme Leader, rulings are final and cannot be appealed. Used as political tool against dissident clergy. [Fact — Med]
- **The chain of capture:** Supreme Leader → appoints Head of Judiciary → appoints/dismisses all judges → Head of Judiciary also nominates Supreme Court Chief Justice and Prosecutor-General (both must be mujtahids) → Guardian Council members (6 of 12) nominated by judiciary head. The judiciary is not captured by the Supreme Leader despite constitutional design; it is captured *by* constitutional design. [Inference — High confidence, structural]

**Phase 3 (1994): The Yazdi Destruction**

- 1994: Head of Judiciary Ayatollah Mohammad Yazdi dissolved the entire remaining modern court structure established in the 1930s. Replaced it with a system modeled on traditional sharia courts. [Fact — IHRDC]
- The 1994 Law of Public Courts established a single-judge system in which the adjudicator was commonly a cleric. Single judges exercised general jurisdiction over all claims and litigation. [Fact — IHRDC]
- Prosecutors’ offices abolished nationwide — judges now functioned as both prosecutor and adjudicator simultaneously, in direct violation of the principle of judicial impartiality. [Fact — IHRDC]
- Appeals became a privilege at the discretion of the issuing court, not a right. [Fact — IHRDC]
- Result: appeals of first-instance judgments rose to 70% of trial court rulings between 1994 and 2004 — reflecting massive public dissatisfaction with arbitrary single-judge verdicts. [Fact — IHRDC]
- Yazdi’s successor, Shahroudi (1999–2009), described the institution he inherited as being in “ruins.” He reversed some of the worst 1994 reforms: reintroduced multi-level courts (2002) and restored the Office of the Public Prosecutor (2004). But the fundamental structure — Supreme Leader control, clerical judges, revolutionary courts, no real independence — remained intact. [Fact — Med, multiple sources]

**Personnel:**

- ~89,000 total judiciary employees (D6.3): Central administration 53,000, Prisons Organization 17,500, Deeds Registration 12,000. [Fact — Med]
- Head of Judiciary, Chief Justice of Supreme Court, and Prosecutor-General must all be Shia mujtahids (high-ranking clerics). Constitutional requirement. [Fact — High]
- All judges must be male, “faithful to the system of the Islamic Republic,” and trained in Islamic law. [Fact — Encyclopaedia Iranica]
- Women banned from serving as full judges. Since mid-1990s, women permitted as deputy prosecutors and (since ~2007) advisory judges in family courts — but cannot sign verdicts. Estimated ~8% of judges are women in some limited capacity. ~1,900 women have qualifications to serve but are excluded. [Fact — IranWire, Iran International]
- Many judges lack formal legal education; appointed from IRGC, Basij, or seminary backgrounds based on ideological loyalty and security clearance rather than legal competence. [Fact — Iran-HRM]
- “Judge’s knowledge” (elm-e qazi) doctrine: In certain cases, a judge can rule based on personal conviction rather than evidence — effectively unlimited judicial discretion. [Fact — Med]
- Two parallel bodies of lawyers: (1) Iran Bar Association Union (SCODA) — partially independent, ~70,000+ qualified lawyers; and (2) Center of Attorneys (Article 187) — created 2001 under judiciary control, ~20,000+ members. The Center of Attorneys does not require the bar exam and must seek annual re-accreditation from the judiciary, undermining political independence. [Fact — American Bar Association, Wikipedia]

### D16.4) The Judiciary as Execution Machine
Iran holds the world’s highest per-capita execution rate. The judiciary is the instrument.

- **2024:** At least 975 people executed — 17% increase over 2023. Only ~10% officially announced. [Fact — IHRNGO, UN OHCHR]
- **2025 (through November):** At least 1,791 executions — an 86% increase over the prior year. November 2025 alone: 336 executions, the highest monthly figure recorded. [Fact — Med, HRANA, Iran-HRM]
- **Post-January 2026:** Judiciary declared all “rioters” to be tried for moharebeh (enmity against God) — a capital offense. Death sentences reported being issued. [Fact — UN OHCHR experts statement]
- **Disproportionate impact on minorities:** Baluch (2–6% of population) comprised 32% of April 2025 executions. Kurds accounted for 52% of all executions for political/group affiliation 2010–2024. [Fact — IHRNGO, DPIC]
- **Drug offenses:** ~48–50% of all executions. Many executed for marginal involvement (e.g., transporting 600 grams for ~$100). [Fact — IHRNGO, Iran-HRM]
- **Women:** At least 61 women executed Jan–Dec 2025 (~70% increase). Several were victims of child marriage who killed abusive husbands. [Fact — Iran-HRM, NCRI]
- **Political function:** Executions spike during periods of regime fragility — post-protest, post-military strikes, succession uncertainty. The judiciary serves as the legal laundering system for state violence. [Inference — High confidence]

### D16.6) What Remains: Survivable Elements
Despite 47 years of destruction, some elements survive that a successor government could build on:

- **The Bar Association lives.** After being shut down (1980), partially reinstated (1984), effectively shut down again (1991–1997), and placed under judiciary supervision, the Iran Bar Association continues to exist with ~70,000+ lawyers. It has demonstrated resilience and commitment to independence. [Fact — IBA/CSHR November 2025 report]
- **Legal education exists.** Iran has law faculties at major universities producing graduates trained in both Islamic and civil law. Many understand international legal standards. The problem is not absence of legal knowledge but its systematic subordination to political authority. [Inference — Med]
- **Commercial and civil law retains secular elements.** The Islamic Republic layered sharia onto the pre-existing French civil law system rather than wholly replacing it. Commercial law, contract law, and significant portions of civil procedure still bear secular DNA. This is recoverable institutional infrastructure. [Fact — Med, multiple sources]
- **Diaspora legal talent.** Iranian-American Bar Association alone has 1,500+ members across eight US chapters. Broader diaspora includes thousands of Iranian-trained lawyers in Europe, Canada, and elsewhere. Many trained in both Iranian and Western legal systems. [Fact — IABA]
- **The “forgiveness movement” and abolition activism.** Inside Iran, despite everything, there exists a documented movement around “No Death Penalty Tuesdays” and a forgiveness movement where victims’ families choose blood money over execution. This signals that even within the current system, alternative justice norms have roots. [Fact — IHRNGO 2024 report]

### D16.7) Transition Implications: The Judiciary Reconstruction Problem
**This is the single hardest institutional reconstruction task in any transition scenario — harder than the military, harder than the economy.**

**1. The bootstrapping problem is absolute.** You need a functioning judiciary to establish rule of law. You need rule of law to have a legitimate judiciary. You cannot build one without the other. Every other transition task — property rights, foreign investment, transitional justice, human rights protections — depends on a judiciary that does not yet exist. [Inference — High confidence]

**2. The personnel gap is catastrophic.** 47 years of clerical appointments means there is no bench of secular-trained judges waiting to be restored. The Davar precedent (1926) — dissolve everything and rebuild from scratch — is the closest historical analogy, but Davar had French judicial experts to help and no population of 88 million expecting functioning courts during the transition. The personnel problem has three layers:

- **Senior judiciary:** Almost entirely Haqqani-pipeline or ideologically vetted clerics. Mostly unsalvageable for a secular system. Many personally implicated in human rights violations.
- **Mid-level judges:** Mixed. Some are competent legal professionals operating within constraints. Some are IRGC/Basij appointees with minimal legal training. Sorting requires case-by-case vetting.
- **Junior/administrative staff:** Mostly civil servants. Largely retainable with institutional reform. [Inference — Med]

**3. Revolutionary courts must be abolished immediately — but the capacity gap is real.** Revolutionary courts have no constitutional basis (established by Khomeini’s edict, not legislation). Their abolition is legally straightforward. But they handle a significant caseload including drug trafficking and organized crime — transferring this to reconstituted public courts requires capacity that doesn’t exist on day one. [Inference — Med]

**4. The “Iraq de-Baathification” lesson applies with extra force.** Wholesale purge of all judiciary personnel creates a legal vacuum that criminal networks, warlords, and vigilantes fill. Iraq’s post-2003 collapse of judicial institutions contributed directly to the chaos that followed. Iran’s judiciary employs ~89,000 people — blanket dismissal is operationally impossible and socially destructive. A lustration model (case-by-case vetting with presumption of retention for non-political personnel) is the only viable approach, and it takes years. [Inference — High confidence, comparative]

**5. The Bar Association is the critical transition asset.** The ~70,000 independent lawyers (plus ~20,000 judiciary-affiliated attorneys) represent the largest pool of legal professionals not directly compromised by judicial appointments. The Bar Association’s institutional memory of independence — dating to 1953 — gives it legitimacy that no other judicial institution possesses. A transition plan should prioritize immediately restoring full Bar Association independence and using it as the institutional backbone for judicial reconstruction. [Inference — Med-High]

**6. Women must return to the bench.** The ban on female judges is constitutionally embedded in the Islamic Republic’s system. Its reversal is both a human rights imperative and a practical necessity — ~1,900 qualified women are immediately available, and female law graduates outnumber males in recent years. This is one transition step where moral imperative and practical need align perfectly. [Inference — High confidence]

**7. The execution machinery must stop on day one.** Iran’s execution rate (1,537–1,791 in 2025 alone) makes an immediate moratorium on executions the most urgent judicial transition action. This is both a human rights necessity and a signal of institutional rupture — the new system defines itself by what it stops doing first. Commuting existing death sentences to imprisonment pending case review is a necessary companion measure. [Inference — High confidence]

**8. Transitional justice and reconstruction are in tension.** The population demands accountability for judicial crimes (1988 massacres, 2019 killings, 2022 crackdowns, January 2026 massacres). The judiciary that committed these crimes cannot try itself. But international tribunals are slow, domestic capacity is absent, and the population’s D14 culture of distrust toward legal institutions makes any judicial process suspect. A hybrid model — international judges sitting alongside vetted Iranian jurists, with Bar Association oversight — may be the only approach that has both capacity and legitimacy. [Inference — Med, comparative]
