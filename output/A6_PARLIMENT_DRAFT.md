# PILLAR A: A6) PARLIAMENT (MAJLIS) — EXPANDED DRAFT



## _L11) A6.1 Historical Context: What the Majlis Was
Iran's parliament is not a post-revolutionary invention — it is the oldest legislative body in the Middle East, predating Turkey's Grand National Assembly by 14 years. [Fact — High]

- **1906 Constitutional Revolution:** Iran's first National Consultative Assembly (Majles-e Showra-ye Melli) convened October 7, 1906, product of a mass constitutionalist movement against Qajar absolutism. 156 members, drawn from bazaar merchants, ulama, and reformers. Produced Iran's first constitution within months. [Fact — High]
- **1906–1908:** Mohammad Ali Shah bombarded the parliament with Russian and British military support (June 1908), dissolved the constitution. Constitutionalists marched on Tehran and restored it in 1909. The cleric Sheikh Fazlollah Nouri, who issued fatwas declaring parliamentarians "apostates," was hanged as a traitor — a verdict the Islamic Republic later reversed, rehabilitating Nouri as a hero. [Fact — High. The rehabilitation is analytically significant: the regime's ideological lineage traces to the anti-parliamentary faction of the Constitutional Revolution, not to its defenders.]
- **1906–1979 arc:** The Majlis functioned continuously (with interruptions) across Qajar and Pahlavi eras. Key moments: Mossadegh's nationalization of oil via parliamentary mandate (1951), the Shah's manipulation and eventual neutering of the institution. By 1979, the Majlis had a 73-year institutional history — imperfect, often manipulated, but a real legislature with genuine debates, competing parties, and constitutional standing. [Fact — Med]
- **Transition relevance:** Iran has deeper parliamentary tradition than any Arab state and deeper than Turkey's. This institutional memory matters: Iranians do not need to be taught what a parliament is. They need one that works. [Inference — High]

## _L22) A6.2 Post-1979 Constitutional Architecture: The Managed Legislature
The Islamic Republic did not abolish parliament — it enveloped it. The 1979 constitution created a unicameral Islamic Consultative Assembly (Majles-e Showra-ye Eslami) with 270 seats (later expanded to 290), retaining the form of elected representation while constructing a theological veto architecture above it. [Fact — High]

### _L26) A6.2.1 Constitutional Powers (on paper)
- **Legislative authority:** Can draft and pass legislation on all matters. Government bills require Council of Ministers approval before submission. [Fact]
- **Executive oversight:** Can interpellate (formally question) ministers, reject cabinet nominees, impeach individual ministers, and — with two-thirds majority — impeach the president. [Fact]
- **Budget authority:** Reviews and amends annual budget. [Fact]
- **Investigation:** Can investigate any matter of national concern (with major exceptions — see §A6.2.2). [Fact]
- **Speaker:** Elected by MPs, sits on key councils of state including the Supreme Council of Cultural Revolution and the transitional leadership council (per Article 111, activated upon Supreme Leader incapacity/death). [Fact — High]


### _L34) A6.2.2 Constitutional Constraints (in practice)
Every parliamentary power is subordinated to three chokepoints, all controlled by the Supreme Leader:

**Chokepoint 1 — Guardian Council candidate vetting (upstream):**

- All candidates must be approved by the Guardian Council before they can run. The Council uses "approbatory supervision" (nezarat-e estesvabi) — meaning it decides who is *fit* to run, not merely who meets formal qualifications. No requirement to explain rejections. [Fact — High]
- In 2024: ~48,000 registered; ~15,200 approved (~32%). Reformists Front boycotted entirely. 27 sitting MPs disqualified. Result: candidates compete within a pre-approved ideological bandwidth. [Fact]
- Historical veto rate on legislation: 27–40% of parliamentary bills rejected by Guardian Council, depending on the parliament's composition. [Fact — Encyclopaedia Iranica]

**Chokepoint 2 — Guardian Council legislative veto (downstream):**

- Article 96: Guardian Council holds absolute veto over all legislation. Six clerics (appointed by Supreme Leader) vote on Islamic compliance; all 12 vote on constitutional compliance. No law has legal force without Guardian Council approval. [Fact — High]
- The Majlis "has no legal status without the Constitutional Council" — this is Iran's own constitutional language. [Fact]

**Chokepoint 3 — Supreme Leader "state order" intervention:**

- The Supreme Leader can directly order legislation removed from the Majlis docket. Most notorious case: mid-2000, Khamenei ordered a press reform bill killed before debate. [Fact — USIP]
- The Majlis cannot investigate institutions under the Supreme Leader's control (state media, IRGC, Leader's Office) without his permission. [Fact — USIP]

**Escape valve — Expediency Discernment Council:**

- Created by Khomeini in 1988 to break legislative deadlock between Majlis and Guardian Council. If the Majlis passes a bill and the Guardian Council rejects it twice, Majlis can (by two-thirds vote) send it to the Expediency Council for final arbitration. [Fact]
- In practice: the Expediency Council is appointed by the Supreme Leader, meets at his order, and functions as another regime-controlled body. It has occasionally sided with parliament on economic legislation. [Fact — Med]

**Net assessment:** The Majlis is a *managed debate chamber* — not a legislature in the democratic sense. It can delay, embarrass, and occasionally block executive action. It cannot challenge the theocratic superstructure. The Guardian Council's upstream vetting ensures the range of permissible debate is set before a single ballot is cast. [Inference — High]

### _L80) A6.3.2 Internal Factional Map
The 12th Majlis is not a monolith — it is a three-way competition *within* the conservative camp:

**1. Paydari Front (Steadfastness Front) — hardline eschatological faction**

- Top Tehran vote-getters: Mahmoud Nabavian (597,770), Amir Hossein Sabeti (487,982), Hamid Rasaei (485,711). All outperformed Speaker Ghalibaf. [Fact]
- Sabeti (b. 1988) — new generation: TV commentator turned first-term MP, vocal JCPOA critic. [Fact]
- Institutional capture: drove Hijab and Chastity Law (2023), pushed Internet Protection Bill, led impeachment of Pezeshkian's Economy Minister, forced out VP Zarif. [Fact]
- Parliamentary minority in absolute numbers but disproportionately influential through ideological discipline and willingness to use obstruction. [Fact — Iran International, Stimson]

**2. Ghalibaf/neo-conservative bloc — pragmatic establishment**

- Speaker: Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf (re-elected May 2024 with 198 votes, re-elected again May 2025). Former IRGC officer, former Tehran mayor, presidential candidate. Connected to Khamenei through wife's family. [Fact]
- Deputy Speakers: Hamid-Reza Haji Babaee (1st), Ali Nikzad (2nd) — both Ahmadinejad-era ministers. [Fact]
- Ghalibaf came *fourth* in Tehran in 2024 (447,905 votes) — down from *first* in 2020 (1.26M). His declining vote share within the conservative base signals loss of ground to Paydari. [Fact — Iran Primer]
- Current posture (Jan 2026): Ghalibaf positioning himself as "chief overseer" rather than partner — threatening cabinet impeachments, distancing from economic failure, building a base for future political contest. [Fact — Iran International, Dec 2025–Jan 2026]

**3. Traditional principlists / Islamic Coalition / miscellaneous conservatives**

- The Coalition Council of Islamic Revolutionary Forces (SHANA) and other traditional right factions. Dominant in numerical terms but lacking the ideological discipline of Paydari or Ghalibaf's institutional network. [Med]


### _L103) A6.3.3 Minority Seats
Five reserved seats for officially recognized religious minorities:

- 1 Zoroastrian
- 1 Jewish
- 1 Assyrian Christian
- 2 Armenian Christian (north and south)

These seats have existed since 1906. They provide token representation — no policy influence. Baha'is, Sunni Muslims (despite being ~10% of population), and unrecognized minorities have no reserved seats. [Fact — Med]

## _L116) A6.4 Key Legislative Actions (2023–2026)
The 11th and 12th Majlis have produced legislation that reveals the institution's function as an *amplifier of hardline policy*, not a check on it:

### _L120) A6.4.1 Hijab and Chastity Law (2023–2024)
- Drafted by judiciary (Apr 2023), expanded by parliament's Judiciary Committee from 15 to 74 articles. Passed September 20, 2023 (152-34 vote). Guardian Council approved September 2024. [Fact]
- Imposes fines up to 360M rials, prison up to 10 years, travel bans, internet bans. Empowers MOIS, IRGC intelligence, judiciary intelligence, police, Basij, and "Command of Enjoining Good and Forbidding Wrong" to enforce. Mandates AI-assisted surveillance via traffic cameras. [Fact — HRW, RFE/RL, CNN]
- President Pezeshkian voiced opposition but acknowledged his signing is "largely ceremonial." Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf announced enforcement would begin December 13, 2024. SNSC suspended implementation amid protest concerns. [Fact]
- **Analytical note:** This law passed *after* the Woman, Life, Freedom movement killed ~551 protesters. The parliament's response to the largest women's rights movement in Iranian history was to *escalate* legal persecution. This is not legislative failure — it is legislative function as designed. [Inference — High]


### _L127) A6.4.2 Nuclear Weapons Letter (September–October 2025)
- 39 MPs signed initial letter (October 2024) calling on SNSC to review defense doctrine and consider nuclear weapons capability. [Fact]
- Expanded to 71+ MPs by September 2025 after UN snapback sanctions. Letter argued Khamenei's fatwa prohibits *use* but not *construction and possession* as deterrent. Cited Quranic verse on preparing "whatever you are able of power" against enemies. [Fact — Kurdistan24, BBC Persian, Iran Front Page]
- MP Hajideligani: NPT withdrawal plan "finalized and ready to be presented to parliament." MP Bakhshayesh Ardastani: "better that we move quickly toward building a nuclear weapon." [Fact — Tasnim, NCRI]
- Parliament debated the letter on September 26, 2025. [Fact — Bloomberg]
- **Analytical note:** This is the first time in Islamic Republic history that a quarter of parliament publicly called for nuclear weapons. The theological reinterpretation — primary ruling (permanent) → secondary ruling (temporary, circumstantial) — mirrors the eschatological faction's broader method of using Islamic jurisprudence to override prior constraints. [Inference — Med. CROSS-REFERENCE: → ITB-A8 §A8.1, ITB-A10 §A10.3]


### _L135) A6.4.3 Impeachment Weapon
- March 2025: Parliament impeached Economy Minister Abdolnasser Hemmati, six months after Pezeshkian took office, citing rial collapse and food price increases. [Fact]
- Forced resignation of VP for Strategic Affairs (Zarif). [Fact]
- December 2025–January 2026: Ghalibaf escalated to threatening full cabinet impeachment if prices not controlled. Deputy Speaker Nikzad noted if >50% of cabinet removed, government loses quorum. [Fact — Iran International]
- **Function:** Impeachment is not oversight — it is factional warfare. Parliament uses its constitutional tools to weaken pragmatist executives while insulating the Supreme Leader and IRGC from accountability. [Inference — Med-High]


### _L142) A6.4.4 Protest Response (January 2026)
- During January 2026 protests, parliament session featured hardliners shouting "Death to America" from the dais. Ghalibaf framed the crisis as a "four-front war" (economic, psychological, military, terrorist). [Fact — Wikipedia/multiple]
- No MP called for restraint against protesters. No legislation to address underlying economic grievances. No investigation of security force conduct. [Fact — absence of contrary evidence in all sources reviewed]
- **Analytical note:** When millions took to the streets, the Majlis had nothing to say to them — only about them. [Inference — High]

## _L150) A6.5 What the Majlis Actually Does (Functional Assessment)
Despite being a managed body, the Majlis serves several real regime functions:

|Function                  |Mechanism                                                                     |Beneficiary                          |
|--------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
|**Legitimation theater**  |Elections create appearance of popular sovereignty                            |Regime international image           |
|**Intra-elite bargaining**|Factional competition channeled into institutional form                       |System stability                     |
|**Executive harassment**  |Interpellation, impeachment threaten ministers                                |Factions seeking power redistribution|
|**Policy amplification**  |Hardline legislation (hijab, internet, nuclear) originates or is expanded here|Paydari/IRGC agenda                  |
|**Pressure release**      |Debates, even heated ones, substitute for genuine opposition                  |Prevents elite defection             |
|**Succession positioning**|Speaker sits on transitional council (Art. 111)                               |Ghalibaf's personal ambition         |

What the Majlis does **not** do:

- Represent public opinion (41% turnout, pre-vetted candidates)
- Check Supreme Leader, IRGC, or judiciary power
- Pass legislation opposed by Guardian Council
- Investigate security force abuses
- Address structural economic or governance reform

[Inference — High confidence. Supported by constitutional analysis, legislative record, and USIP/Chatham House assessments]

## _L175) A6.6 Transition Relevance
### _L177) A6.6.1 The Paradox
The Majlis is simultaneously the most democratic institution in the Islamic Republic and a demonstration of why managed democracy is not democracy. Abolishing it would remove the only venue for even constrained public representation. Preserving it perpetuates an institution designed to prevent genuine legislative sovereignty. [Inference — High]


### _L181) A6.6.2 Transition Implications
1. **Guardian Council abolition is the single highest-leverage transition act.** Every other institutional reform flows from whether the candidate vetting mechanism is removed. A Majlis without Guardian Council vetting is a fundamentally different institution — potentially a genuine legislature overnight, using existing procedures, existing building, existing staff. [Inference — High]
1. **Institutional continuity advantage over judiciary.** Unlike the judiciary (D16), which was *replaced* root-and-branch with clerics, the Majlis retains procedural infrastructure: committee system, interpellation process, budget review, impeachment protocol. The procedures are intact even if the personnel are captured. Personnel can be replaced through free elections; procedures cannot be easily rebuilt from nothing. [Inference — High]
1. **Speaker's constitutional role in succession.** Under Article 111, if the Supreme Leader dies or is incapacitated, a three-person transitional council assumes power: President + Judiciary Chief + one Guardian Council faqih chosen by the Expediency Council. However, the Majlis Speaker sits on several state councils and any transitional arrangement would require parliamentary cooperation. Ghalibaf's positioning for this role is explicit. [Fact/Inference — Med]
1. **Election infrastructure exists.** Iran has conducted 12 parliamentary elections since 1980. Polling stations, voter rolls, regional constituencies — the infrastructure for free elections exists physically. The obstacle is the vetting superstructure, not the electoral machinery. [Inference — Med-High]
1. **Proportional representation reform already legislated.** The 12th Majlis passed a proportional representation law for future elections (effective for next cycle). Under this model, organized parties with disciplined voter bases benefit most. In a transition, this could either help (if new parties emerge rapidly) or entrench (if former regime factions reorganize fastest). [Fact/Inference — Med]
1. **First free Majlis election as legitimation mechanism.** A genuinely free parliamentary election — not presidential, not constitutional assembly, but a *Majlis* election — would be the simplest way to create a body with democratic legitimacy that could then mandate further reforms. The institutional form already exists; only the vetting needs to change. This is faster than building a constituent assembly from scratch. [Inference — Med]
1. **Risk: empty parliament problem.** If transition happens abruptly, sitting MPs may flee, refuse to serve, or be arrested. The 12th Majlis's near-total hardliner composition means very few current members would survive even a basic vetting for participation in a transitional legislature. Unlike the judiciary, where at least some judges handle civil/commercial law, virtually every current MP was pre-selected for ideological loyalty. [Inference — Med]
1. **Minority representation framework needs expansion.** The five reserved minority seats (1906 framework) exclude Baha'is, Sunni Muslims (~10% of population), and other unrecognized communities. Any post-transition Majlis must address this structural exclusion. [Inference — Med]

## _L223) A6.9 Key Findings Summary
1. **Iran's parliament is 120 years old** — the oldest in the Middle East. This institutional memory is a transition asset.
1. **The Islamic Republic enveloped the Majlis rather than abolishing it** — unlike the judiciary, the institutional form survived even as the content was captured.
1. **The Guardian Council is the chokepoint** — removing it transforms the Majlis from managed theater to potential legislature overnight.

1. **Fastest path to legitimate transitional body** — a free Majlis election, using existing infrastructure minus Guardian Council, is the lowest-friction path to democratic legitimacy.

## _L248) A6.10.1 The Mechanism
Iran's managed electoral system contains a self-tightening feedback loop in which declining public participation systematically amplifies the most extreme faction's power:

```
Low turnout → disciplined ideological base overrepresented →
hardline legislation → public alienation → lower turnout →
more hardline capture → repression escalation → massacre →
even lower turnout → near-total hardline capture
```

Each cycle narrows the electorate closer to the regime's committed voter base (~25% of eligible voters per Stimson Center estimate). At that floor, elections become an intra-regime selection process — the public is no longer a variable. [Inference — High]

## _L274) A6.10.2 Why the Massacre Changes the Calculus
The January 2026 massacre (12,000–18,000 killed per CBS/Iran International estimates, 21,000+ arrested per regime data from June 2025 precedent) transforms every input variable:

**On the population side (demand for participation collapses):**

- Boycott sentiment was already overwhelming at 41% turnout *before* anyone was killed. After the largest massacre in Iranian contemporary history, the emotional and physical barriers to voting are categorically higher. [Inference — High]
- The 553,000 blank/invalid ballots in Tehran in 2024 — deliberate protest voters — are now far more likely to stay home entirely, or be dead, imprisoned, intimidated, or in exile. [Inference — Med-High]
- Former President Khatami abstained in 2024 (first time ever). After the massacre, the entire reformist establishment has zero credibility left to mobilize even reluctant voters. The argument "vote to change from within" is dead. [Inference — High]
- Practical obstacles multiply: internet blackout precedent means the regime can cut communications during any future election campaign period; mass arrests deplete the pool of potential opposition organizers; mourning cycles (40-day Chehelom) create competing social mobilization that draws energy away from regime-sanctioned processes. [Inference — Med]

**On the candidate side (supply of moderates collapses):**

- Who registers as a "moderate" candidate after this? Pezeshkian — the last reformist safety valve — was powerless to prevent the massacre, powerless to block the hijab law, and faces cabinet-wide impeachment threats. His presidency is the definitive proof that reformism within the system is futile. [Inference — High]
- The Guardian Council already disqualified 68% of candidates in 2024. Post-massacre, the vetting will be *tighter* — any candidate who expressed sympathy for protesters, criticized the crackdown, or advocated restraint becomes a security risk in the Guardian Council's calculus. [Inference — Med-High]
- Self-censorship effect: even candidates who pass vetting will calibrate their rhetoric further rightward. The cost of being perceived as "soft" increased by orders of magnitude in January 2026. [Inference — Med]

**On the regime's internal calculus (reward structure shifts toward maximalists):**

- The faction that *demanded* maximum force (Paydari) was proven "right" in regime survival terms — the regime survived, protests were crushed. The faction that hesitated or wanted dialogue (Pezeshkian, remnant pragmatists) is discredited *within the elite*. [Inference — Med-High]
- Khamenei has historically used the Guardian Council to maintain factional *balance within the right* — vetoing reformists to prevent challenge from below, but also constraining any single conservative faction from monopolizing power. Post-massacre, this balancing incentive weakens. When a regime has just killed thousands to survive, it rewards the killers, not the hesitators. [Inference — Med]
- IRGC and judiciary — Paydari's institutional allies — executed the crackdown. Their political capital within the system is at a peak. Denying Paydari candidates Guardian Council approval after the IRGC just saved the regime would be a factional insult the system may not risk. [Inference — Med]

## _L297) A6.10.3 Paydari vs. Ghalibaf: The Real Contest
The next Majlis election is not hardliners vs. moderates — that contest ended in 2024. The real fight is **Paydari vs. Ghalibaf's neo-conservative bloc** for control of the hardline supermajority.

**Paydari advantages post-massacre:**

- Ideological vindication ("only force works")
- IRGC/Basij institutional alignment (the forces that carried out the crackdown)
- Organized voter base: heyat networks, Basij families, IRGC dependents — the most disciplined turnout machine in Iranian politics
- New proportional representation law favors organized parties over personality-based candidacies — Paydari is the most organized faction [Fact — Iran International]
- Financial independence via Mahsouli's personal fortune [Fact — per A10.3.2]

**Ghalibaf advantages:**

- Incumbency and Speaker's institutional power
- Khamenei's historical preference for balance (but see A6.10.2 on why this may weaken)
- Some IRGC factions (Khatam al-Anbiya economic network) aligned with pragmatic conservatism rather than eschatological maximalism
- Personal positioning as "competent manager" rather than ideologue

**Projection:** Paydari captures a larger share of the next Majlis than 2024, potentially approaching a working majority. Whether they capture the Speakership depends on Khamenei's intervention. If Khamenei is dead or incapacitated by 2028, no one constrains them. [Inference — Med]

## _L318) A6.10.4 Consequences for Transition Planning
### _L320) Consequence 1: Majlis-as-transitional-vehicle becomes less viable
A6.6.2 (finding #6) identified a free Majlis election as the fastest path to a legitimate transitional body. This assumed a Majlis that, once the Guardian Council vetting was removed, could be repurposed. A 90%+ Paydari parliament cannot be repurposed. Any transition that occurs while the 13th Majlis (2028–2032) is sitting would need to **bypass parliament entirely** — creating a parallel constituent assembly or national council rather than working through existing legislative infrastructure. [Inference — Med-High]

This weakens one of the Majlis's key transition advantages (institutional continuity) while preserving another (election infrastructure can still be used for a *new* body elected under new rules).


### _L326) Consequence 2: Nuclear weapons advocacy becomes legislative program
In 2025, 71 MPs (~24%) called for nuclear weapons. In a Paydari-dominated parliament, this becomes a *majority position*. The Majlis could:

- Pass formal legislation directing the executive to weaponize
- Legislate NPT withdrawal
- Create budget lines for weapons development
- Use interpellation/impeachment to punish any minister who resists

This creates a constitutional confrontation: the Supreme Leader's fatwa vs. parliamentary legislation. Under normal circumstances, the Guardian Council would block such laws as inconsistent with the Leader's stated position. But if the eschatological faction has captured the Guardian Council *and* the Majlis, the fatwa itself becomes the target — reinterpretable as a "secondary ruling" subject to circumstantial revision. [Inference — Med. CROSS-REFERENCE: → ITB-A8 §A8.1 (doctrinal flexibility), ITB-A10 §A10.3 (fatwa reinterpretation)]


### _L337) Consequence 3: Ghalibaf's succession positioning becomes existential
If Paydari captures the Speakership, Ghalibaf loses:

- His Article 111 role in the transitional leadership council
- His institutional platform for the presidential ambition
- His ability to position as "system manager" — the role goes to a Paydari figure

This makes the period between now and the next parliamentary election a critical window for Ghalibaf. He must either:

- **Align with Paydari** (abandon pragmatist positioning, compete on their terms)
- **Seek Khamenei's direct protection** (which requires demonstrating loyalty that may further implicate him in post-massacre accountability)
- **Make a move before the next election** — use the current Speakership to position for the presidential race or the succession itself before Paydari can displace him

[Inference — Med. CROSS-REFERENCE: → ITB-A11 §Pinochet mechanism (Ghalibaf as potential alignment-switch figure)]


### _L353) Consequence 4: The ratchet's terminal state
The ratchet has a logical endpoint: an election in which turnout falls below 20%, the entire candidate pool is Paydari-aligned, and the Majlis becomes indistinguishable from a Paydari party congress. At that point:

- Electoral legitimation — the original purpose of the republican institutions — ceases to function even as theater
- The regime loses its last claim to popular sovereignty
- The Majlis becomes a rubber stamp for the eschatological faction's agenda rather than the Supreme Leader's

This terminal state may be reached by 2028. It represents the **completion of the process the Guardian Council began** — transforming elections from a managed competition into a selection mechanism for a single faction. The irony is that the Guardian Council was designed to protect the system from external challenge; instead, it enabled internal capture by the system's most extreme element. [Inference — Med-High]
